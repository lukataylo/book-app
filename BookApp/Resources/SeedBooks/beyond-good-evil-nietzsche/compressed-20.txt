# CHAPTER V. THE NATURAL HISTORY OF MORALS

The moral sentiment in Europe is subtle and refined, while the so-called Science of Morals remains coarse and initial—an interesting contrast. What is genuinely needed is modest: the collection and classification of the vast domain of moral sentiments, as preparation for a theory of types of morality. Instead, philosophers have always demanded more—they wanted to give morality a foundation, treating morality itself as something "given." Because they knew moral facts only as arbitrary epitomes of their own environment, they never even glimpsed the real problems, which only disclose themselves through comparison of many moralities. Every philosophical "grounding" of morality has proved merely a learned expression of faith in prevailing morality—the reverse of genuine questioning. Schopenhauer, for instance, sought the foundation in "injure no one, help all you can"—a proposition absurd and sentimental in a world whose essence is Will to Power. That a pessimist and repudiator of the world should halt at morality and play the flute to laede-neminem ethics—is that really a pessimist?

Every system of morals is a sign-language of the emotions. When someone asserts "there is a categorical imperative in us," the interesting question is what this reveals about the asserter. Some systems justify their author before others; some tranquilize him; some are instruments of revenge, concealment, or self-glorification. Kant's morality says essentially: "What is estimable in me is that I know how to obey—and with you it shall not be otherwise."

Every system of morals is a tyranny against nature and reason—but this is no objection. What is essential and invaluable in every system is that it is a long constraint. Freedom, elegance, boldness, masterly certainty in thought, art, and conduct have only developed through the tyranny of arbitrary law. The artist knows that his most natural condition is not letting himself go but strict obedience to a thousand precise laws. The essential thing is long obedience in the same direction—from this results virtue, art, music, reason, spirituality. The violence of Christian intellectual discipline educated the European spirit, gave it its remorseless curiosity and subtle mobility, even as much strength was stifled in the process. Nature is indifferent and extravagant in her magnificence. The moral imperative of nature is: "Thou must obey someone, and for a long time; otherwise thou wilt come to grief."

Industrious races find idleness a hardship; the English instinct to hallow Sunday so that the worker hungers for his weekday again is a masterly intercalated fast. Whole generations infected with moral fanaticism are similarly such periods of restraint, during which an impulse learns to humble, purify, and sharpen itself. It was under the pressure of Christian constraint that the sexual impulse sublimated into love.

Socratism—the notion that all evil is done unwittingly and that knowledge of the good necessarily produces it—savours of the populace, who see only the unpleasant consequences of wrongdoing. Plato did all he could to interpret something noble into his teacher's tenets, but the Socratic mode of reasoning is essentially utilitarian and plebeian in origin.

The old problem of faith versus knowledge, instinct versus reason, first appeared with Socrates, who took the side of reason with his dialectical talent, yet secretly laughed at himself when he found the same incapacity in his own instincts. His real falseness was self-outwitting—following instinct while persuading reason to supply good arguments. Plato wished to prove that reason and instinct lead spontaneously to the good; since Plato, instinct—what Christians call faith, what I call the herd—has triumphed in moral matters.

Our senses learn late and never completely to be subtle organs of knowledge. We hear strange music badly; we involuntarily form new impressions into familiar shapes. We fabricate the greater part of experience and can hardly contemplate any event except as inventors thereof. From remote ages we have been accustomed to lying—or, more politely, we are much more of an artist than we are aware of.

What we experience in dreams pertains at last just as much to the belongings of our soul as anything actually experienced. The man who has often flown in dreams will find "happiness" differently coloured in his waking hours, and the poets' descriptions of flight will seem too earthly and muscular compared with his own.

The difference among men shows itself not only in their lists of desirable things but in what they regard as actually possessing a desirable thing. One man is satisfied with a woman's bodily surrender; another demands that she give up everything for him; a third wishes to be known in his full devilry before feeling truly possessed. Among the charitable, one finds an awkward craftiness that first constructs the needy person as one who merits help and will be grateful—charity as a desire for property. Parents make something like themselves out of their children and call it education.

The Jews performed the miracle of the inversion of valuations: their prophets fused "rich," "godless," "wicked," and "violent" into one term of reproach, and coined "world" as a condemnation. With the word "poor" made synonymous with "saint," the slave-insurrection in morals commences.

The beast of prey—Caesar Borgia—is misunderstood so long as one seeks morbidness in the constitution of these healthiest of tropical growths. Moralists hate the virgin forest and discredit the tropical man. This belongs to the chapter: "Morals as Timidity."

All systems of morals addressed to "happiness" are suggestions for behaviour adapted to the degree of danger individuals present to themselves—recipes for passions, expediencies permeated with old-wife wisdom, grotesque in their generalization and unconditional tone. Whether Stoic coldness toward emotion, Spinoza's destruction of emotions by analysis, Aristotelian moderation, or the wanton surrender taught by Hafis and Goethe—expediency, expediency, expediency, mixed with stupidity, stupidity, stupidity. This also for the chapter: "Morals as Timidity."

Because obedience has been most practiced among mankind, the need for it is now innate as a formal conscience demanding "Thou shalt." This need fills its form with whatever content commanders—parents, teachers, laws, public opinion—shout into its ear. The herd-instinct of obedience is transmitted best, at the cost of the art of command. Where this instinct reaches its extreme, commanders suffer from bad conscience and must deceive themselves into thinking they merely execute higher orders. This is the moral hypocrisy of the commanding class in Europe today. Meanwhile the gregarious man glorifies his gentle, herd-useful qualities as the peculiarly human virtues, and representative constitutions attempt to replace commanders with the summing together of clever gregarious men. Yet what a blessing the appearance of an absolute ruler is for these Europeans—the history of Napoleon's influence is almost the history of the higher happiness the century attained in its worthiest individuals.

The man of an age of dissolution, with contrary instincts struggling in him, is on average a weak man who seeks happiness as a soothing medicine—repose, undisturbedness, final unity, the "Sabbath of Sabbaths." But should the conflict in such natures operate as an additional incentive, and should they also possess mastery in carrying on the conflict with themselves, there arise those enigmatical men predestined for conquering others—Alcibiades, Caesar, Frederick II, Leonardo da Vinci—who appear precisely in the same periods as the weaker type, complementary to it.

As long as utility is only gregarious utility, there can be no morality of love to one's neighbour—sympathetic actions are then ultra-moral, neither praised nor blamed as moral. Love of neighbour is always secondary, partly conventional, a manifestation of fear of one's neighbour. Once society is secured against external danger, the strong instincts formerly honoured as necessary—love of enterprise, revengefulness, rapacity, love of power—are felt as doubly dangerous and gradually branded as immoral. The gregarious instinct draws its conclusions: whatever elevates the individual above the herd is called evil; mediocrity of desires attains moral distinction. At a point of diseased mellowness, society takes the part of the criminal against punishment. The imperative of the timidity of the herd is: "We wish that some time there may be nothing more to fear!"—and this is called progress.

Morality in Europe at present is herding-animal morality—only one kind of human morality, beside which higher moralities are or should be possible. This morality defends itself with all its strength, saying "I am morality itself." The democratic movement is the inheritance of the Christian movement. The anarchist dogs roving through European culture are at one with democrats and socialists in their hostility to every form of society other than the autonomous herd, in their distrust of punitive justice, in their religion of sympathy, in their deadly hatred of suffering, in their belief in the community as deliverer.

We who hold a different belief fix our hopes in new philosophers—minds strong and original enough to initiate opposite estimates of value, to transvalue eternal valuations, forerunners who will fix the constraints compelling millenniums to take new paths. To teach man the future of humanity as his will, to put an end to the frightful rule of folly and chance called "history"—for this a new type of philosopher and commander will be needed. The universal degeneracy of mankind to the level of the gregarious pigmy with equal rights is undoubtedly possible. He who has thought this out to its conclusion knows another loathing unknown to the rest—and perhaps also a new mission.

# CHAPTER VI. WE SCHOLARS

The declaration of independence of the scientific man from philosophy is one of the subtler after-effects of democratic disorganization. Science, having resisted theology, now proposes to lay down laws for philosophy and play the philosopher on its own account. The specialist stands defensively against synthetic tasks; the utilitarian sees in philosophy only a series of refuted systems; the fear of mysticism and the disregard of individual philosophers extends to disregard of philosophy generally. Most frequently behind the proud disdain of philosophy in young scholars lies the evil after-effect of some particular philosopher—as Schopenhauer's unintelligent rage against Hegel severed a generation of Germans from German culture. Philosophy reduced to a "theory of knowledge," rigorously denying itself the right to enter—that is philosophy in its last throes.

The dangers besetting the evolution of the philosopher are manifold: the sciences have grown so vast that the philosopher grows tired as a learner, or specializes and never attains his superspection. Or he gets aloft too late, impaired and coarsened. His intellectual conscience makes him hesitate; he dreads becoming a dilettante. He demands from himself a verdict not concerning science but concerning life and the worth of life—and must seek his way through the most extensive and painful experiences. The genuine philosopher lives "unphilosophically" and imprudently, risks himself constantly, plays this bad game.

The scientific man has always something of the old maid about him—not conversant with the two principal functions of man. He is a non-ruling, non-authoritative type; industrious, patient, equable, with the instinct for people like himself. He is full of petty envy, with a lynx-eye for the weak points in natures to whose elevations he cannot attain. The worst thing of which a scholar is capable is the Jesuitism of mediocrity, which labours instinctively for the destruction of the exceptional man—to relax every bent bow, with confiding sympathy, which is the real art of Jesuitism.

The objective man is a mirror—no purpose in himself. He waits until something comes and then expands sensitively. Whatever personality he still possesses seems to him accidental and disturbing. He does not take himself seriously; he is serene not from lack of trouble but from lack of capacity for grasping his trouble. His mirroring soul no longer knows how to affirm or deny, command or destroy. He is an instrument, something of a slave—the sublimest sort, but nothing in himself. He is not a model man, not a commencement or primary cause, nothing hardy and self-centred that wants to be master—rather a soft, delicate potter's-form waiting for content to shape itself thereto. Consequently, also, nothing for women.

When a philosopher nowadays makes known that he is not a skeptic, people hear it impatiently and regard him as dangerous. Skepticism is the most spiritual expression of a certain physiological temperament called nervous debility, arising whenever races or classes long separated suddenly blend. In the new generation everything is disquiet, doubt, and tentativeness; the will is most diseased. Europe is skeptical in all its heights and depths—paralysis of will, bedecked and disguised as "objectiveness," "the scientific spirit," "l'art pour l'art." The disease of the will is worst where civilization has longest prevailed. In Russia, by contrast, the power to will has been long stored up and waits threateningly to be discharged. Rather than desiring Russia's dissolution into parliamentary imbecility, I would prefer such an increase in Russia's threatening attitude that Europe would have to acquire one will—a new caste ruling the Continent with aims set thousands of years ahead. The time for petty politics is past; the next century brings the struggle for dominion of the world.

The new warlike age may favour a stronger kind of skepticism—the skepticism of daring manliness exemplified by Frederick the Great, which despises and yet grasps, undermines and takes possession, gives the spirit a dangerous liberty while keeping strict guard over the heart. This German form of skepticism, as continued Fredericianism risen to highest spirituality, kept Europe for a considerable time under the dominion of the German critical and historical spirit.

The philosophers of the future will be experimenters and critics in a new, more dangerous sense—not skeptics but men certain as to standards of worth, employing a unity of method, standing alone, capable of self-responsibility, delighting in denial and dissection with a considerate cruelty that knows how to handle the knife even when the heart bleeds. They will be sterner than humane people desire. They will smile when someone says "That thought elevates me, why should it not be true?" They will not want to be called critics, for critics are instruments of the philosopher, not philosophers themselves.

Philosophical workers—after the pattern of Kant and Hegel—fix and formalize existing bodies of valuations, making the past conspicuous and manageable. The real philosophers, however, are commanders and law-givers: they say "Thus shall it be!" They determine the Whither and Why of mankind; their knowing is creating, their creating is law-giving, their will to truth is Will to Power.

The philosopher has always found himself in contradiction to the day in which he lives; his enemy has always been the ideal of his day. In putting the vivisector's knife to the breast of the very virtues of their age, philosophers have been the bad conscience of their time—for the sake of a new greatness of man. In a world of "modern ideas" that confines everyone to a specialty, the philosopher would place greatness precisely in comprehensiveness and multifariousness, determining worth by the extent to which a man can stretch his responsibility. Nowadays the taste of the age weakens the will; consequently in the ideal of the philosopher, strength of will and capacity for prolonged resolution must be central. He shall be greatest who can be most solitary, most divergent, the man beyond good and evil, master of his virtues and of superabundance of will.

# CHAPTER VII. OUR VIRTUES

We Europeans of the day after tomorrow—with our dangerous curiosity, our multifariousness, our mellow and sweetened cruelty in sense and spirit—shall presumably have only those virtues which accord with our most secret inclinations. This "believing in one's own virtues" is practically the same as what was formerly called a good conscience—that respectable pigtail our grandfathers hung behind their heads. We are still their worthy grandchildren in this one respect.

Our actions shine alternately in different colours, determined by different moralities, seldom unequivocal. We learn to despise when we love, and precisely when we love best—unconsciously, without ostentation, with the shame and secrecy of goodness. Morality as attitude is opposed to our taste nowadays, as religion as attitude finally became opposed to the taste of our fathers.

The psychologists of France have never exhausted their enjoyment of bourgeois stupidity—Flaubert, the honest citizen of Rouen, tasted nothing else in the end, as his mode of self-torment. More interesting, however, is the unconscious astuteness with which good, fat, honest mediocrity always behaves towards loftier spirits—a subtle, Jesuitical astuteness a thousand times subtler than the understanding of the middle class at its best. Study the philosophy of the "rule" in its struggle with the "exception": there you have a spectacle fit for gods.

The practice of moral judgment and condemnation is the favourite revenge of the intellectually shallow on those who are less so—an indemnity for being badly endowed, and an opportunity for acquiring spirit, since malice spiritualises. They need the belief in God for the "equality of all before God." I would rather flatter them with the theory that lofty spirituality is the uThe historical sense—the capacity for divining quickly the order of rank of valuations by which a people has lived—is the sixth sense the nineteenth century recognized as its specialty, coming to us in the train of the democratic mingling of classes and races. Our instincts run back in all directions; we are a kind of chaos. We have secret access everywhere, including the labyrinth of imperfect civilizations—and since most of human civilization has been semi-barbarity, the historical sense implies almost the sense for everything, which immediately proves it an ignoble sense. We enjoy Homer as the French of the seventeenth century could not. We accept Shakespeare's wild motleyness with secret cordiality as a refinement reserved expressly for us. Yet what is most difficult for us to grasp and love is precisely the perfection and ultimate maturity in every culture—the essentially noble, the moment of halcyon self-sufficiency, the goldenness and coldness of things that have perfected themselves. Proportionateness is strange to us. Our itching is for the infinite, the immeasurable. Like the rider on his forward-panting horse, we let the reins fall before the infinite—and are only in our highest bliss when we are in most danger.

Hedonism, pessimism, utilitarianism, eudaemonism—all modes of thinking that measure worth according to pleasure and pain—are naïvetés which every one conscious of creative powers will look down upon with scorn, though not without sympathy. We see how man dwarfs himself, how you dwarf him! You want to do away with suffering; we would rather have it increased. Well-being as you understand it is an end that renders man ludicrous and contemptible. The discipline of great suffering alone has produced all the elevations of humanity hitherto. In man creature and creator are united: there is not only clay, mire, and chaos, but also the sculptor, the hardness of the hammer, the divinity of the spectator. Your sympathy applies to the creature in man—to that which must suffer and is meantto suffer. Ours resists your sympathy as the worst of all pampering. So it is sympathy against sympathy. There are higher problems than the problems of pleasure and pain.

We immoralists are woven into a strong net of duties and cannot disengage ourselves. Occasionally we dance in our chains; more often we gnash our teeth. But fools say of us: "These are men without duty."

Honesty, granting it is the virtue of which we free spirits cannot rid ourselves—let us labour at it with all our perversity and love. And if it should grow weary and would fain have things pleasanter, let us remain hard, we latest Stoics, and send to its help whatever devilry we have in us: our disgust at the clumsy and undefined, our love of adventure, our intellectual Will to Power. Every virtue inclines to stupidity, every stupidity to virtue. Let us be careful lest out of pure honesty we eventually become saints and bores.

All moral philosophy hitherto has been tedious and has belonged to the soporific appliances. It is desirable that as few people as possible should reflect upon morals. The indefatigable English utilitarians stalk on in the footsteps of Bentham, who stalked in the footsteps of Helvetius—no new thought, no finer turning of an old thought, an impossible literature unless leavened with mischief. The old English vice called cant, moral Tartuffism, has insinuated itself into these moralists under the form of the scientific spirit. They want English morality recognized as authoritative, and the striving after English happiness—comfort and fashion—declared the true path of virtue. They cannot grasp that the "general welfare" is a nostrum, that what is fair to one may not be fair to another, that there is a distinction of rank between man and man, and consequently between morality and morality.

Almost everything we call "higher culture" is based upon the spiritualizing and intensifying of cruelty. The "wild beast" has not been slain—it lives, it flourishes, it has only been transfigured. That which constitutes the painful delight of tragedy is cruelty; that which operates in tragic sympathy, and at the basis of everything sublime up to the highest thrills of metaphysics, obtains its sweetness from the intermingled ingredient of cruelty. There is abundant enjoyment even in one's own suffering, in causing one's own suffering. The seeker of knowledge operates as an artist of cruelty in that he compels his spirit to perceive against its own inclination—forces it to say Nay where he would like to affirm and love. In every desire for knowledge there is a drop of cruelty.

That imperious something called "the spirit" wishes to be master internally and externally. Its power to appropriate foreign elements reveals itself in a strong tendency to assimilate the new to the old, to simplify the manifold, to overlook the absolutely contradictory. Its object is the feeling of growth, the feeling of increased power. This same will has at its service an apparently opposed impulse: a preference for ignorance, a closing of windows, an acceptance of obscurity—as much as is necessary according to the degree of its digestive power. Counter to the propensity for appearance and simplification, there operates the sublime tendency of the man of knowledge, which takes things profoundly and thoroughly—a kind of cruelty of the intellectual conscience. He will say: "There is something cruel in the tendency of my spirit." In effect, to translate man back into nature; to master the many vain interpretations scratched over the eternal original text, *homo natura*; to bring it about that man shall stand before man as he now stands before other forms of nature, with fearless Oedipus-eyes and stopped Ulysses-ears, deaf to the enticements of old metaphysical bird-catchers who have piped: "Thou art more! thou art higher! thou hast a different origin!"—this is a strange and foolish task, but who can deny it is a task?

Learning alters us. But at the bottom of our souls there is something unteachable, a granite of spiritual fate, of predetermined decision. In each cardinal problem there speaks an unchangeable "I am this"; a thinker cannot learn anew about man and woman, but can only follow to the end what is fixed about them in himself. In view of this, permission may be granted me to utter some truths about "woman as she is"—merely my truths.

Woman wishes to be independent and therefore begins to enlighten men about "woman as she is"—one of the worst developments of the general uglifying of Europe. Woman has so much cause for shame: so much pedantry, superficiality, petty presumption, and indiscretion concealed in her, which has been restrained hitherto by the fear of man. Female voices are already raised which make one afraid. Is it not in the worst taste that woman sets herself up to be scientific? Enlightenment has hitherto been men's affair. Woman does not want truth—what does woman care for truth? Her great art is falsehood; her chief concern is appearance and beauty. We men honour and love this very art and instinct in woman. That which inspires respect in woman, and often enough fear also, is her nature, which is more "natural" than that of man—her genuine, carnivora-like cunning flexibility, her tiger-claws beneath the glove, her naïveté in egoism, her untrainableness and innate wildness. To be mistaken in the fundamental problem of "man and woman," to deny the profoundest antagonism and the necessity for an eternally hostile tension, to dream of equal rights and equal claims—that is a typical sign of shallow-mindedness.

The weaker sex has in no previous age been treated with so much respect by men as at present—this belongs to the tendency of democracy. They want more, they learn to make claims; the tribute of respect is at last felt to be galling; rivalry for rights would be preferred. Woman is losing modesty—and losing taste. She is unlearning to fear man. Since the French Revolution the influence of woman in Europe has declined in proportion as she has increased her rights and claims. The "emancipation of woman," insofar as it is desired by women themselves, proves to be a symptom of the increased weakening of the most womanly instincts. That which inspires respect in woman is her nature—her force of will. The most powerful and influential women in the world had just to thank their force of will, not their schoolmasters, for their power over men.

# CHAPTER VIII. PEOPLES AND COUNTRIES

Richard Wagner's overture to the Mastersinger is a piece of magnificent, heavy, latter-day art which presupposes two centuries of music as still living in order to be understood. It flows broad and full; it has fire and courage, and at the same time the loose skin of fruits which ripen too late. All in all, however, no beauty, no South, nothing of southern clearness, no grace, no dance, hardly a will to logic—something German in the best and worst sense: manifold, formless, inexhaustible, a real token of the German soul, which is at the same time young and aged, too ripe and yet still too rich in futurity. They belong to the day before yesterday and the day after tomorrow—they have as yet no today.

We "good Europeans" also have hours when we allow ourselves a warm-hearted patriotism, a relapse into old loves and narrow views. Duller spirits may perhaps only get done with what confines its operations in us to hours—in a considerable time: some in half a year, others in half a lifetime. A statesman who rears up for the masses a new Tower of Babel, some monstrosity of empire and power, they call "great." But what if a statesman condemned his people to "practise politics" when they had something better to do, stimulated their slumbering passions, made their minds narrow and their tastes "national"—would he be great? "Undoubtedly! Otherwise he could not have done it! It was mad perhaps—but perhaps everything great has been just as mad at its commencement!" "Misuse of words! Strong and mad—not great!"

Behind all the moral and political formulas of "civilization," "humanizing," and "progress"—behind the democratic movement in Europe—an immense physiological process goes on: the assimilation of Europeans, their increasing detachment from the conditions under which united races originate, the slow emergence of an essentially super-national and nomadic species of man who possesses a maximum of the art of adaptation. This process will probably arrive at results on which its naïve propagators would least care to reckon. The same conditions that produce a levelling and mediocrising of man are in the highest degree suitable to give rise to exceptional men of the most dangerous and attractive qualities. The democratising of Europe is at the same time an involuntary arrangement for the rearing of tyrants—taking the word in all its meanings, even in its most spiritual sense.

There was a time when Germans were called "deep" by way of distinction. The German soul is above all manifold, varied in its source, aggregated and superimposed rather than actually built. As a people made up of extraordinary mixing and mingling of races, the Germans are more intangible, more contradictory, more unknown, more incalculable, more surprising, and even more terrifying than other peoples are to themselves—they escape definition, and are thereby the despair of the French. The German soul has passages and galleries, caves and dungeons; its disorder has much of the charm of the mysterious. The German does not exist—he is becoming, he is "developing himself." "Development" is therefore the essentially German discovery in the domain of philosophical formulas. "Good-natured and spiteful"—such a juxtaposition, preposterous in the case of every other people, is unfortunately too often justified in Germany. The German drags at his soul, he drags at everything he experiences; he digests his events badly and never gets "done" with them. German depth is often only a difficult, hesitating "digestion." This confidingness, this showing-the-cards of German honesty, is probably the most dangerous and most successful disguise which the German is up to nowadays—his proper Mephistophelean art. Let us continue to honour its appearance and good name, and not barter away our old reputation as a people of depth for Prussian smartness and Berlin wit. It is wise for a people to pose and let itself be regarded as profound, clumsy, good-natured, honest, and foolish: it might even be—profound to do so.

The "good old" time sang itself out in Mozart—how happy are we that his Rococo still speaks to us, his tender enthusiasm, his courtesy of heart, his longing for the elegant and amorous. Beethoven is the intermediate event between an old mellow soul constantly breaking down and a future over-young soul always coming; over his music is spread the twilight of eternal loss and eternal extravagant hope. German music that came afterwards belongs to Romanticism—a movement historically shorter, more fleeting, and more superficial than the great interlude from Rousseau to Napoleon. With Robert Schumann, German music was threatened with its greatest danger: that of losing the voice for the soul of Europe and sinking into a merely national affair.

How indignantly a reader with a third ear stands beside the slowly turning swamp of sounds without tune and rhythms without dance which Germans call a "book"! How lazily, how badly the German reads! How many Germans know that there is art in every good sentence—art which must be divined if the sentence is to be understood? The masterpiece of German prose is therefore with good reason the masterpiece of its greatest preacher: the Bible has hitherto been the best German book. Compared with Luther's Bible, almost everything else is merely "literature."

Every nation has its own Tartuffery and calls that its virtue.

What Europe owes to the Jews includes above all one thing of the nature both of the best and the worst: the grand style in morality, the fearfulness and majesty of infinite demands, the whole Romanticism and sublimity of moral questionableness—and consequently the most attractive and exquisite element in those iridescences and allurements to life, in the aftersheen of which the evening sky of our European culture now glows—perhaps glows out. For this, we artists among the spectators and philosophers are grateful to the Jews.

About the Jews: I have never yet met a German who was favourably inclined to them. However decided the repudiation of actual anti-Semitism on the part of prudent men, this prudence is not directed against the nature of the sentiment itself, but only against its dangerous excess. The Jews are beyond all doubt the strongest, toughest, and purest race at present living in Europe; they know how to succeed even under the worst conditions by means of virtues which one would nowadays label as vices—owing above all to a resolute faith which does not need to be ashamed before "modern ideas." A thinker who has the future of Europe at heart will calculate upon the Jews as among the surest and likeliest factors in the great play and battle of forces. The Jews, if they desired it, could now have the ascendancy over Europe—that they are not working and planning for that end is equally certain. They rather wish to be insorbed and absorbed by Europe, to be finally settled and respected somewhere, to put an end to the wandering Jew—and one should make advances to this impulse, for which purpose it would perhaps be useful to banish the anti-Semitic bawlers out of the country.

The English are not a philosophical race. Bacon represents an attack on the philosophical spirit generally; Hobbes, Hume, and Locke represent an abasement of the idea of a "philosopher" for more than a century. It is characteristic of such an unphilosophical race to hold on firmly to Christianity—they need its discipline for moralizing and humanizing. The Englishman is more gloomy, sensual, headstrong, and brutal than the German—and for that very reason more pious. That which offends even in the humanest Englishman is his lack of music, to speak figuratively and literally: he has neither rhythm nor dance in the movements of his soul and body.

The German soul has corridors and inter-corridors; it is confused, growing, still partly unformed. The German himself is becoming, and therefore is not yet. The French, English, and Italians are more finished national types; the German still lacks a culture of his own and compensates with a certain capacity for mediation and comprehension. I dream of a super-European music, akin to the palm-tree and the desert, knowing nothing of good and evil, hospitable to the last fugitives of a sinking moral world.

The nationality-craze blinds observers to the most unmistakable sign of the age: Europe wishes to be one. Napoleon, Goethe, Beethoven, Stendhal, Heine, Schopenhauer, Wagner—the real tendency of such men was to prepare the European of the future. Wagner belongs with the later French Romantics: the same storm, the same hunger for expression at any cost, the same virtuosity beyond genius, the same final collapse at the Christian cross. His Siegfried is perhaps the most remarkable creation—too free, too healthy, too anti-Catholic for mellow Latin taste. But in old age Wagner preached the way to Rome, and Parsifal betrays what he became: Rome's faith by intuition.

# CHAPTER IX. WHAT IS NOBLE?

Every elevation of the type "man" has been the work of an aristocratic society—one believing in a long scale of rank and requiring slavery in some form. Without the pathos of distance between classes, the further pathos of self-surmounting within the soul could never arise. The truth about aristocracy's origin is hard: barbarian men of prey threw themselves upon weaker, more peaceful races. The noble caste was always first the barbarian caste; their superiority was psychical—they were more complete men, which also means more complete beasts.

Corruption means different things in different organizations. A healthy aristocracy does not regard itself as a function of the state but as its highest justification—it accepts with good conscience that a legion of individuals must be suppressed so that the select few may rise, like the Sipo Matador vine that strangles an oak in order to unfold its crown in open light.

To refrain from injury and exploitation and call this a social principle is a will to the denial of life. Life itself is essentially appropriation, injury, conquest of the weak, incorporation—exploitation belongs to the nature of living being as a primary organic function, a consequence of the will to power. Every living organization must do to other bodies what its members refrain from doing to one another.

In touring the moralities of the earth two primary types emerge: master-morality and slave-morality. In master-morality the rulers determine "good" as the exalted, proud disposition; "bad" means despicable—the cowardly, the lying, the servile. The noble man is a creator of values; he honours what he recognizes in himself; his happiness is the feeling of overflow and power. He honours the hard-hearted, is proud of not being made for sympathy, reveres age and tradition, and acknowledges duties only to his equals.

Slave-morality arises among the oppressed. Here sympathy, patience, humility, and friendliness are the virtues—they ease the burden of existence. The "evil" man in slave-morality is the dangerous, powerful one who arouses fear; the "good" man is the safe, harmless one—eventually tinged with stupidity. The desire for freedom and happiness belongs to slave-morality as reverence and devotion belong to the aristocratic. Love as passion is of noble, Provençal origin.

Vanity is difficult for a noble man to understand. The ordinary man has always been only what he passed for; he waits for an opinion about himself and submits to it. Vanity is an atavism—the slave's instinct of subjection breaking through.

A species becomes stable through long struggle with unfavourable conditions. In an aristocratic commonwealth hardness, uniformity, and intolerance in education forge a type. When the tension finally relaxes—when enemies vanish and abundance arrives—variations explode: magnificent growth and simultaneous decay, egoisms warring for sun and light, the individual thrown back on his own law-giving. At this point moral philosophers preach mediocrity, the only morality that still has a hearing—though they can never avow what they desire.

The instinct for rank is itself the sign of high rank. Vulgarity spurts up like dirty water before holy things; nobility falls silent. The best example of European discipline is the reverence maintained for the Bible—books of such depth require the tyranny of authority to protect them across millennia.

What ancestors have done is indelibly in the constitution; education can only deceive about inherited plebeianism. Egoism belongs to the essence of a noble soul—the unalterable belief that other beings must naturally be in subjection. The noble soul accepts this without harshness, as justice itself; among equals it moves with assurance and delicate respect, for every star is a similar egoist.

The communicability of common experience has been the most potent force shaping mankind—it drives everything toward the similar, the ordinary, the gregarious, the ignoble. The psychologist who studies higher men finds their ruin to be the rule; he needs sternness and cheerfulness more than anyone, lest sympathy suffocate him. Success is the greatest liar; the "great man" is a fiction composed afterwards. Those great poets—Byron, Musset, Poe, Leopardi—were men of the moment, light-minded, often taking revenge with their works for an internal defilement, finally shattering at the Christian cross.

Profound suffering makes noble; it separates. Epicurism is one refined disguise of this suffering—gaiety used as camouflage by those who would be misunderstood.

Signs of nobility: never to lower one's duties to duties for everybody; to count one's prerogatives among one's duties. The noble soul has reverence for itself—this is the fundamental certainty not to be sought, found, or lost.

Every philosophy is a foreground philosophy; behind every cave is a deeper cave. Every deep thinker fears being understood more than being misunderstood—the former wounds his heart.

What is noble? Not actions, which are always ambiguous; not works. It is a fundamental certainty which a noble soul has about itself. The noble soul has reverence for itself.

The genius of the heart—that tempter-god, born rat-catcher of consciences—imposes silence on the self-conceited, smoothes rough souls, scents hidden treasure, and sends everyone away richer in themselves, more uncertain, more delicate, broken up, full of hopes that lack names. This questionable god is Dionysus, the great equivocator, whose philosophy I have learned from mouth to mouth as his last disciple. He said: "I love mankind—man is an agreeable, inventive animal. I think how I can advance him further—make him stronger, more evil, and more profound. Also more beautiful." One sees that it is not only shame this divinity lacks. In some things the gods could come to us for instruction. We men are—more human.

Alas, my written and painted thoughts! Not long ago you were young and malicious, full of thorns and secret spices. Now you are ready to become truths—so immortal-looking, so pathetically honest, so tedious. We immortalize only what is exhausted and mellow, only the afternoon of things. But nobody will divine how you looked in your morning, you sudden sparks of my solitude, you old, beloved—evil thoughts!
