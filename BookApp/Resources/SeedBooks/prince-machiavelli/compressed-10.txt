# INTRODUCTION

Nicolo Machiavelli was born at Florence on 3rd May 1469, second son of a lawyer, of old Florentine nobility. His life divides into three periods matching three eras of Florentine history: youth under Lorenzo de' Medici's magnificence; public service under the free Republic, 1494–1512; and literary retirement after the Medici returned and dismissed him. He died 22nd June 1527, never regaining office.

His official career as Secretary to the Ten of Liberty and Peace brought him into contact with Catherina Sforza, Louis XII, Cesare Borgia, Julius II, Ferdinand of Aragon, and the Emperor Maximilian—the living material of The Prince. Imprisoned and tortured in 1512, released through Leo X, he retired to San Casciano. In a famous letter of December 1513 he describes entering his study each evening, putting off peasant clothes, donning court dress, and conversing with the ancients—from which commerce he distilled the "little volume on Principalities." The Prince was never published by him; its text remains disputable; Lorenzo de' Medici, to whom it was finally dedicated, apparently never read it.

Machiavelli was a man of great observation and industry, less successful as a practising statesman than as a writer. His literary works include the Discourses on Livy, Mandragola, the Art of War, and the History of Florence. The Prince endures because it addresses the eternal problems between rulers and ruled, and because its truths—that men are dupes of simplicity and greed, that crimes may win empire but not glory, that necessary wars are just—remain demonstrable at every turn.

# DEDICATION

To the Magnificent Lorenzo di Piero de' Medici: I offer not horses or jewels but knowledge of great men's actions, gained through long experience and continual study of antiquity. I have not embellished this work with swelling words, wishing the truth and weightiness of the subject to make it acceptable. As those who draw landscapes stand in the plain to see mountains, and on mountains to see plains, so to understand princes one must be of the people, and to understand the people one must be a prince. I suffer a great and continued malignity of fortune; I trust your Magnificence will recognise my extreme desire for your greatness.

# THE PRINCE

# CHAPTER I. HOW MANY KINDS OF PRINCIPALITIES THERE ARE, AND BY WHAT MEANS THEY ARE ACQUIRED

All states are either republics or principalities. Principalities are hereditary or new. New ones are either entirely new, like Milan to Francesco Sforza, or annexed to an existing state, like Naples to Spain. Such dominions are acquired by one's own arms or those of others, by fortune or by ability.

# CHAPTER II. CONCERNING HEREDITARY PRINCIPALITIES

Hereditary states are easier to hold than new ones. A prince of average powers need only not transgress ancestral customs and deal prudently with circumstances. The Duke of Ferrara withstood Venice and Julius II solely because of long establishment. The hereditary prince has less cause to offend, is more loved, and in the continuity of his rule the memory and motive for change are lost.

# CHAPTER III. CONCERNING MIXED PRINCIPALITIES

Men change rulers willingly, hoping to better themselves, and are deceived, finding they have gone from bad to worse. A new prince must burden his acquisition with soldiers and hardships, making enemies of all he injures while unable to satisfy those who helped him. Louis XII took Milan quickly and lost it quickly, because those who opened the gates to him found their hopes deceived. Retaken provinces are less easily lost a second time, since the prince can punish delinquents and strengthen weak places.

When annexed territories share language and customs, it suffices to extinguish the old ruling family and alter neither laws nor taxes; they soon become one body with the old state. Where language and customs differ, the surest remedies are: reside there in person; send colonies to one or two key places—cheap, injuring only a minority who are scattered and powerless; make yourself protector of weaker neighbours and weaken the stronger; allow no powerful foreigner to gain a footing.

The Romans observed all these rules in Greece—keeping the Achaeans and Ætolians friendly, humbling Macedonia, driving out Antiochus—because they dealt with troubles while they were still curable. Like hectic fever, political evils are easy to cure when hard to detect, and hard to cure when easy to detect. They knew war cannot be avoided, only deferred to another's advantage.

Louis XII committed five errors: he destroyed the minor powers; increased the Church's strength; brought in Spain; did not reside in Italy; sent no colonies. A sixth error—ruining Venice—removed the one counterweight that might have restrained the others. He assisted Alexander VI to occupy the Romagna, weakening himself while aggrandising the Church; then divided Naples with Spain, introducing a rival where he had been sole arbiter. A blunder to avoid war only defers it to your disadvantage.

# CHAPTER IV. WHY THE KINGDOM OF DARIUS DID NOT REBEL AGAINST ALEXANDER'S SUCCESSORS

States are governed either by a prince with servants, or by a prince and barons holding dignity by ancient blood. The Turkish empire exemplifies the first: hard to conquer because no discontented baron can invite you in, but easy to hold once the ruling family is destroyed. France exemplifies the second: easy to enter by winning over a malcontent baron, but hard to hold because the surviving lords generate endless fresh movements.

Darius's kingdom resembled the Turkish model; Alexander had only to defeat him in the field, and the state remained secure. The frequent rebellions against Rome in Spain, France, and Greece arose from the many local principalities there; once the memory of these faded, Rome held them securely.

# CHAPTER V. CONCERNING CITIES THAT LIVED UNDER THEIR OWN LAWS BEFORE ANNEXATION

Three courses exist: ruin the city; reside there in person; let it live under its own laws, drawing tribute and installing a friendly oligarchy. The Spartans held Athens and Thebes by oligarchy and lost them. Rome dismantled Capua, Carthage, and Numantia and did not lose them. A city accustomed to freedom never forgets the watchword of liberty—as Pisa after a hundred years under Florence. The safest course is to destroy such cities or to reside in them.

# CHAPTER VI. CONCERNING NEW PRINCIPALITIES ACQUIRED BY ONE'S OWN ARMS AND ABILITY

A wise man follows paths beaten by great men and imitates the supreme, so that if his ability falls short, it will at least savour of theirs—like archers who aim above the mark to reach it. Moses, Cyrus, Romulus, and Theseus owed nothing to fortune beyond opportunity; without their ability the opportunity would have come in vain, and without the opportunity their powers would have been extinguished.

Nothing is more difficult than introducing a new order of things: the innovator has for enemies all who prospered under the old, and only lukewarm defenders among those who may prosper under the new. Hence all armed prophets have conquered and unarmed ones been destroyed—as happened to Savonarola the moment the multitude ceased to believe in him. Those who overcome the ascent are afterwards secure, respected, and happy.

# CHAPTER VII. CONCERNING NEW PRINCIPALITIES ACQUIRED BY THE ARMS OF OTHERS OR BY FORTUNE

Those who rise by fortune alone have little trouble rising but much in keeping their position, for their foundations rest on the goodwill and fortune of whoever elevated them—two most inconstant things.

Cesare Borgia acquired his state through his father's ascendancy and lost it when that ascendancy declined, despite taking every measure a wise man could. He first weakened the Orsini and Colonnesi in Rome by absorbing their adherents; then crushed the Orsini at Sinigalia; then governed the Romagna through Ramiro d'Orco, a swift and cruel man, and when order was restored had Ramiro executed and displayed in the piazza at Cesena—satisfying and dismaying the people at once. He planned against a hostile successor to the papacy by exterminating dispossessed lords, winning Roman gentlemen, controlling the College of Cardinals, and acquiring enough power to survive the first shock. Three of the four plans were complete at Alexander's death; only his own mortal illness undid him. He told me on the day Julius II was elected that he had foreseen every contingency at his father's death except that he himself would be dying.

His one error was allowing Julius II's election. He who thinks new favours make great men forget old injuries deceives himself; Julius did not rest until he had ruined Cesare.

# CHAPTER VIII. CONCERNING THOSE WHO OBTAIN A PRINCIPALITY BY WICKEDNESS

Agathocles of Syracuse rose from a potter's son to king by military ability and ruthless massacre of the senate and rich citizens, yet defended his city and forced Carthage to terms. His courage and greatness of mind were admirable; his barbarous cruelty prevents him being celebrated among the most excellent men. Oliverotto da Fermo similarly seized Fermo by inviting his uncle and the leading citizens to a banquet and slaughtering them, then was himself strangled by Cesare Borgia at Sinigalia one year later.

Cruelties may be called well used—if evil may be called good—when applied all at once out of necessity and not repeated. Badly used cruelties multiply with time. Injuries ought to be done all at once so they are tasted less; benefits given little by little so their flavour lasts longer. A prince must also live among his people so that no unexpected circumstance forces him to change course, for harsh measures taken too late are seen as forced and earn no gratitude.

# CHAPTER IX. CONCERNING A CIVIL PRINCIPALITY

Where a private citizen becomes prince by the favour of fellow citizens—neither by wickedness nor by fortune alone but by shrewd management—he rises either through the people or through the nobles. The nobles wish to oppress the people; the people wish not to be oppressed. He who rises through the nobles finds himself surrounded by men who consider themselves his equals; he who rises through the people finds himself alone with none unwilling to obey.

A prince can never secure himself against a hostile people, for they are too many; against hostile nobles he can, for they are few. The worst the people can do is abandon him; hostile nobles will also rise against him. Therefore a prince raised by the nobles must above all win the people; men who receive good from one from whom they expected evil are bound more closely than if they had always been favoured.

These principalities are weakest when passing from civil to absolute government through magistrates rather than personal rule, for in troubled times the magistrates can destroy the government and the prince finds few he can trust. A wise prince arranges that his citizens always need the state and him.

# CHAPTER X. HOW THE STRENGTH OF PRINCIPALITIES OUGHT TO BE MEASURED

A prince who can raise a sufficient army to meet any attacker in the field needs no further discussion. One who cannot must fortify his town well, manage his subjects well, and not attempt to defend the open country. Men are always averse to enterprises where difficulties are visible; a well-fortified town with a prince not hated by his people will not be lightly attacked. If the enemy burns the countryside, the prince should give his subjects hope that the evil will be brief and fear of the enemy's cruelty; and after the burning, the damage is done and there is no remedy, so they unite with him the more closely.

# CHAPTER XI. CONCERNING ECCLESIASTICAL PRINCIPALITIES

Ecclesiastical principalities are acquired by capacity or fortune and held without either, sustained by ancient religious ordinances. Their princes do not defend their states yet are not taken from them; do not rule their subjects yet are not abandoned. I will not discuss what human reason cannot reach.

Yet the Church's temporal greatness deserves explanation. Before Charles VIII, the Italian potentates kept each other in balance, using the Orsini and Colonnesi factions to keep the papacy weak. Alexander VI, using Cesare Borgia and the French entry, broke this balance and accumulated temporal power—though his intention was to aggrandise the duke, not the Church. Julius II inherited this power and added Bologna, humbled Venice, and drove France from Italy. Pope Leo found the pontificate most powerful of all.

# CHAPTER XII. HOW MANY KINDS OF SOLDIERY THERE ARE, AND CONCERNING MERCENARIES

The foundations of all states are good laws and good arms; where arms are good, laws will be good. Arms are one's own, mercenary, auxiliary, or mixed. Mercenaries are useless and dangerous: disunited, ambitious, undisciplined, unfaithful, cowardly before enemies. They have no reason to die for you beyond a trifle of stipend. The ruin of Italy has been caused by reliance on mercenaries for many years—Charles VIII seized Italy with chalk in hand.

Mercenary captains are either capable, in which case you cannot trust them since they aspire to their own greatness, or incapable, in which case you are ruined in the ordinary way. Rome and Sparta stood for ages armed and free. The Switzers are completely armed and quite free. The Carthaginians were oppressed by their own mercenaries after the first war with Rome. Francesco Sforza, hired against Venice, allied with Venice to crush his Milanese masters. From such arms conquests come slowly and losses suddenly and portentously.

# CHAPTER XIII. CONCERNING AUXILIARIES, MIXED SOLDIERY, AND ONE'S OWN

Auxiliaries—another's forces called in to aid you—are even more dangerous than mercenaries: if they lose you are undone, if they win you are their captive. Julius II, having found his mercenaries poor in the enterprise against Ferrara, turned to Ferdinand of Spain's auxiliaries; only extraordinary luck saved him from captivity. The Emperor of Constantinople admitted ten thousand Turks into Greece to oppose his neighbours; they would not leave—the beginning of Greece's servitude.

With mercenaries the danger is cowardice; with auxiliaries, valour. The wise prince has always preferred to lose with his own arms than conquer with others'. Cesare Borgia moved from French auxiliaries to mercenaries to his own men, and his reputation rose at each step. Charles VII of France, having liberated France by his own valour, established ordinances for his own infantry; his son Louis abolished the infantry and enlisted the Swiss, subordinating French arms to foreigners—a mistake whose consequences are now visible.

No principality is secure without its own forces; fame and power not founded on one's own strength are unstable.

# CHAPTER XIV. THAT WHICH CONCERNS A PRINCE ON THE SUBJECT OF THE ART OF WAR

A prince ought to have no other aim or study than war and its rules and discipline—the sole art belonging to him who rules. Francesco Sforza became Duke of Milan through being martial; his sons, avoiding arms, became private persons. An unarmed prince is despised, and there is nothing proportionate between the armed and the unarmed.

In peace a prince should exercise body and mind: body through the chase, learning localities, how mountains rise and valleys open; mind through reading history, studying how illustrious men bore themselves in war, as Alexander imitated Achilles, Caesar imitated Alexander, Scipio imitated Cyrus. He should increase his resources in calm so that fortune finds him prepared in adversity.

# CHAPTER XV. CONCERNING THINGS FOR WHICH MEN, AND ESPECIALLY PRINCES, ARE PRAISED OR BLAMED

Many have imagined republics and principalities never seen in reality. How one lives is so far from how one ought to live that he who neglects what is done for what ought to be done sooner effects his ruin than his preservation. A prince must know how to do wrong and use it or not according to necessity. He need not possess all virtues but must appear to possess them; and he must not fear the reproach of vices without which the state can only be saved with difficulty, for something that looks like virtue may be his ruin, and something that looks like vice may bring him security.

# CHAPTER XVI. CONCERNING LIBERALITY AND MEANNESS

Liberality exercised so as to be known consumes all resources; to maintain the reputation the prince must burden his people, becoming odious and poor. A wise prince should not fear the reputation of meanness: his revenues suffice, he can defend himself, he need not burden his people. We have not seen great things done in our time except by those considered mean. Caesar's liberality was appropriate to one seeking power, not to one who has it. Of that which is neither yours nor your subjects'—the spoils of war—you can be a ready giver; squandering your own injures you. It is wiser to bear reproach without hatred for meanness than reproach with hatred for rapacity.

# CHAPTER XVII. CONCERNING CRUELTY AND CLEMENCY, AND WHETHER IT IS BETTER TO BE LOVED THAN FEARED

A prince should wish to be considered clement, but must not misuse clemency. Cesare Borgia's cruelty reconciled and unified the Romagna; the Florentines, avoiding a reputation for cruelty, permitted Pistoia to be destroyed. A few examples of severity are more merciful than disorders that injure the whole people.

It is safer to be feared than loved when one must choose, because men are ungrateful, fickle, false, covetous; love is preserved by obligation which the baseness of men breaks at every opportunity, while fear is preserved by dread of punishment which never fails. Nevertheless a prince must avoid hatred: he can endure being feared while not hated, which holds as long as he keeps his hands off his subjects' property and women. Men forget the death of a father sooner than the loss of patrimony.

Hannibal held an enormous mixed army in foreign lands through inhuman cruelty combined with boundless valour; without the cruelty his other virtues would not have sufficed—as Scipio's too great forbearance caused his army in Spain to rebel. A prince must establish himself on what is in his own control: he must endeavour only to avoid hatred.

# CHAPTER XVIII. CONCERNING THE WAY IN WHICH PRINCES SHOULD KEEP FAITH

Those princes who have done great things have held good faith of little account and known how to circumvent men by craft. There are two ways of contesting: by law, proper to men; by force, proper to beasts. A prince must use both. He must be fox and lion: the lion cannot defend against snares, the fox cannot defend against wolves. A wise lord cannot keep faith when such observance is turned against him and the reasons for pledging it no longer exist. If men were entirely good this precept would not hold; but they are bad and will not keep faith with you.

A prince must be a great pretender and dissembler; men are so simple and subject to present necessities that he who seeks to deceive will always find someone to deceive. Alexander VI did nothing but deceive men and always found victims. It is unnecessary to have all good qualities but very necessary to appear to have them: appear merciful, faithful, humane, upright, religious—and be so—but have a mind ready to change to the opposite when necessity requires. Men judge by the eye, not the hand; everyone sees what you appear to be, few know what you are. Let a prince have the credit of conquering and holding his state; the means will always be considered honest and he will be praised by everybody.

# CHAPTER XIX. THAT ONE SHOULD AVOID BEING DESPISED AND HATED

Above all a prince must avoid being hated and contemptible. He is hated by being rapacious and violating property and women; contemptible by being fickle, frivolous, effeminate, irresolute. He should show greatness, courage, gravity, and fortitude, and make his judgments irrevocable. A prince highly esteemed is not easily conspired against.

The conspirator faces fear, jealousy, and prospect of punishment; the prince has the majesty of the state, laws, friends, and popular goodwill. When the people are hostile, everything is to be feared; when they are friendly, conspiracies are nearly impossible—as the Bolognese showed when they avenged Annibale Bentivogli and sent to Florence for his heir.

Among the best-governed kingdoms is France, where the parliament beats down the great and favours the lesser without reproach to the king—an excellent arrangement. Princes should leave affairs of reproach to others and keep those of grace in their own hands.

The Roman emperors faced a third difficulty beyond nobles and people: the soldiers, who loved a warlike, cruel, rapacious prince, while the people loved a peaceful one. Those who could not satisfy both were ruined. Marcus succeeded because he inherited by right and his virtues kept both orders in place. Pertinax was overthrown because the soldiers, accustomed to Commodus's licence, could not endure honest life—showing that hatred is acquired as much by good works as bad. Severus, knowing how to counterfeit both fox and lion, kept soldiers friendly while oppressing the people, and his supreme renown protected him. Commodus debased himself by competing with gladiators and was conspired against and killed. Maximinus was despised for his low birth and feared for his cruelty; the whole world conspired against him.
