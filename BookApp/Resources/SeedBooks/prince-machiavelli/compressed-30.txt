# INTRODUCTION

Nicolo Machiavelli was born at Florence on 3rd May 1469, second son of a lawyer, of old Florentine nobility. His life divides naturally into three periods matching three eras of Florentine history. His youth coincided with the greatness of Florence under Lorenzo de' Medici. From 1494, when the Medici fell, until 1512, when they returned, he served the free Republic. From 1512 until his death on 22nd June 1527 he lived in enforced retirement, writing.

Savonarola's influence on the young Machiavelli was slight; he appears in The Prince merely as an example of an unarmed prophet who came to a bad end. Lorenzo's magnificence impressed him deeply, and it is to Lorenzo's grandson that he dedicates The Prince.

# OFFICE — 1494–1512

Appointed Chancellor and Secretary to the Ten of Liberty and Peace, Machiavelli took a leading part in the Republic's affairs. His first mission, to Catherina Sforza in 1499, taught him that earning the confidence of the people is worth more than relying on fortresses. In 1500 he went to France to negotiate with Louis XII, who subsequently committed the five capital errors in statecraft catalogued in The Prince. Much of his official life turned on the ambitions of Pope Alexander VI and Cesare Borgia, whose methods he studied with unflinching attention. He watched Cesare cheated into allowing the election of Julius II, a cardinal who had every reason to fear the duke—confirming his maxim that new favours do not make great men forget old injuries. He observed Ferdinand of Aragon accomplish great things under the cloak of religion, and diagnosed the Emperor Maximilian's repeated failures as the product of secrecy combined with want of resolution.

# LITERATURE AND DEATH — 1512–27

Dismissed by decree of 7th November 1512, accused of conspiracy, imprisoned and tortured, Machiavelli retired to his small property at San Casciano. In a famous letter to Francesco Vettori of 13th December 1513 he describes his evenings: stripping off his peasant clothes, putting on his court dress, and entering the ancient courts of great men through his books—feeding on that food which is his alone, composing a small work on principalities. The Prince was never published by him; its text remains disputable; Lorenzo de' Medici, to whom it was finally dedicated, apparently never read it and gave Machiavelli no employment.

He subsequently wrote the Discourses on Livy, the Art of War, and the History of Florence, the last presented to Giuliano de' Medici, now Pope Clement VII, just as Medicean power was again collapsing. When the Medici were expelled in 1527 Machiavelli hurried back to Florence hoping to recover his secretaryship, fell ill, and died.

# THE MAN AND HIS WORKS

Modern Florence gave him a cenotaph in Santa Croce, recognising that Italy found in his works the idea of her unity. The harsh construction of his doctrine was unknown to his own day. He was a man of great observation and industry, though only moderately successful as a diplomat—misled by Catherina Sforza, ignored by Louis XII, overawed by Cesare Borgia. His literary gift never failed. The Prince deals with eternal problems between rulers and ruled. Its ethics are those of his contemporaries, yet not out of date so long as governments rely on material rather than moral force. Crimes may win an empire but not glory. Necessary wars are just wars. These truths remain.

# DEDICATION

To the Magnificent Lorenzo Di Piero De' Medici

Desiring to present myself to your Magnificence with some testimony of devotion, I have found nothing more precious than knowledge of the actions of great men, acquired by long experience and continual study of antiquity. I have not embellished this little volume with swelling words or rounded periods, for I wished either that no honour be given it, or that the truth of the matter make it acceptable. Just as those who draw landscapes place themselves below to contemplate mountains, and upon mountains to contemplate plains, so to understand the nature of the people one must be a prince, and to understand princes one must be of the people. If your Magnificence will read and consider it, you will learn my extreme desire that you attain the greatness which fortune and your attributes promise.

# THE PRINCE

# CHAPTER I. HOW MANY KINDS OF PRINCIPALITIES THERE ARE, AND BY WHAT MEANS THEY ARE ACQUIRED

All states, all powers that have held rule over men have been either republics or principalities. Principalities are either hereditary or new. New ones are either entirely new, as Milan was to Francesco Sforza, or members annexed to a hereditary state, as Naples was to Spain. Such dominions are acquired either by the arms of the prince himself, or of others, or by fortune, or by ability.

# CHAPTER II. CONCERNING HEREDITARY PRINCIPALITIES

There are fewer difficulties in holding hereditary states long accustomed to a ruling family than new ones. It is sufficient not to transgress ancestral customs and to deal prudently with circumstances. The hereditary prince has less cause to offend and will be more loved; and in the antiquity of his rule the memories that make for change are lost, for one change always leaves the toothing for another.

# CHAPTER III. CONCERNING MIXED PRINCIPALITIES

Difficulties occur in new principalities because men change their rulers willingly, hoping to better themselves, and are afterwards deceived, finding they have gone from bad to worse. The new prince burdens those who submitted with his soldiery and infinite hardships. Thus he has enemies in all those he injured and cannot satisfy those who put him there.

Louis XII quickly occupied Milan and as quickly lost it, because those who had opened the gates found themselves deceived in their hopes. When rebellious provinces are acquired a second time they are not so lightly lost, because the prince takes the opportunity to punish delinquents and strengthen weak places.

When dominions added to an ancient state share the same language and customs, it is enough to extinguish the former ruling family and alter neither laws nor taxes; they quickly become one body with the old principality. When they differ in language, customs, or laws, great energy is needed. The best remedy is for the prince to go and reside there: disorders are seen as they spring up and quickly remedied; officials cannot pillage; subjects have prompt recourse to the prince. The other course is to send colonies to one or two places as keys to the state. Colonies cost little, offend only those from whom land is taken—who, being poor and scattered, cannot hurt—and are more faithful than garrisons, which consume all the income and exasperate the whole state.

The prince in a differing country ought to make himself head of the less powerful neighbours, weaken the more powerful, and prevent any strong foreigner from gaining a footing, for such a one will always be introduced by the discontented. The Romans observed these measures exactly: they sent colonies, maintained friendly relations with minor powers without increasing their strength, kept down the greater, and allowed no strong foreign power authority. They dealt with troubles at once, knowing that war is not avoided but only deferred to the advantage of others.

Louis XII made five errors: he destroyed the minor powers, increased the strength of the Church in Italy, brought in a foreign power, did not settle in the country, and sent no colonies. A sixth error—ruining the Venetians—would have been avoidable had he not first committed the others. From this a general rule: he who causes another to become powerful ruins himself, since that predominancy was brought about by astuteness or force, both of which the newly powerful man distrusts.

# CHAPTER IV. WHY THE KINGDOM OF DARIUS DID NOT REBEL AGAINST ALEXANDER'S SUCCESSORS

Principalities are governed in two ways: by a prince with servants who govern as ministers by his favour; or by a prince and barons who hold dignity by antiquity of blood. The Turk's kingdom exemplifies the first; France the second. The Turk's state is hard to seize—his ministers cannot carry the people with them—but easy to hold once conquered, for there is no established nobility to rally resistance. France is easy to enter by gaining a discontented baron, but hard to hold, because the lords that remain make themselves heads of fresh movements.

Darius's kingdom resembled the Turk's; Alexander had only to defeat him in the field, and the state was secure. Hence the ease with which Alexander held Asia, and the difficulties others have had in keeping acquisitions—arising not from the conqueror's ability but from the want of uniformity in the subject state.

# CHAPTER V. CONCERNING CITIES THAT LIVED UNDER THEIR OWN LAWS BEFORE ANNEXATION

There are three courses for holding such states: ruin them, reside there in person, or permit them to live under their own laws while drawing tribute and establishing a friendly oligarchy. The Spartans held Athens and Thebes by oligarchy and lost them; the Romans dismantled Capua, Carthage, and Numantia and did not lose them. He who becomes master of a city accustomed to freedom and does not destroy it may expect to be destroyed by it, for liberty and ancient privilege are never forgotten—as Pisa showed after a hundred years under Florence. But cities accustomed to a prince, whose ruling family is extinguished, cannot agree on a successor and are easily held.

# CHAPTER VI. CONCERNING NEW PRINCIPALITIES ACQUIRED BY ONE'S OWN ARMS AND ABILITY

A wise man ought always to follow paths beaten by great men and imitate those who have been supreme, so that if his ability does not equal theirs, it will at least savour of it—as archers aim higher than the mark to reach it.

Moses, Cyrus, Romulus, Theseus owed nothing to fortune beyond opportunity, which brought them the material to mould. Without opportunity their powers would have been extinguished; without those powers the opportunity would have come in vain. Those who by valorous ways become princes acquire with difficulty but keep with ease. There is nothing more difficult, more perilous, or more uncertain than to introduce a new order of things, because the innovator has for enemies all who did well under the old conditions, and only lukewarm defenders in those who may do well under the new.

Hence all armed prophets have conquered and unarmed ones been destroyed—as happened to Savonarola, who was ruined the moment the multitude ceased to believe in him, having no means to make the unbelievers believe by force.

# CHAPTER VII. CONCERNING NEW PRINCIPALITIES ACQUIRED BY THE ARMS OF OTHERS OR BY FORTUNE

Those who become princes solely by fortune have little trouble rising but much in keeping their position, for they fly up and have no roots. Francesco Sforza, by proper means and great ability, rose from private person to Duke of Milan and kept what he acquired with little trouble. Cesare Borgia acquired his state during his father's ascendancy and lost it on its decline, notwithstanding that he had done everything a wise and able man should do.

Alexander VI wished to aggrandize the duke but faced many difficulties: he could not make him master of any Church state without offending Milan and Venice; the Orsini and Colonnesi controlled Italian arms. He therefore dissolved Louis XII's marriage, enabling the French to enter Italy; the Pope got soldiers from Louis for the Romagna, which yielded on the king's reputation.

The duke then found two obstacles: unreliable Orsini forces and French goodwill. He weakened the Orsini and Colonnesi in Rome by drawing their adherents to himself with pay and office. When the Orsini perceived that the duke's aggrandizement was their ruin, they met at Magione; the duke overcame the resulting rebellion with French help, then lured the leaders to Sinigalia and destroyed them. Finding the Romagna under weak masters who had plundered rather than ruled it, he appointed Ramiro d'Orco—swift and cruel—to restore order. When order was restored and Ramiro had become odious, the duke had him executed and left on the piazza at Cesena with a bloody knife beside him, satisfying and dismaying the people at once.

At Alexander's death the duke was sick, his state only partly consolidated, two powerful hostile armies on his back. Yet his foundations were so good that the Romagna waited for him more than a month. His single error was allowing the election of Julius II, who had every reason to fear and ruin him. He who believes new benefits make great men forget old injuries deceives himself.

When all the duke's actions are recalled, I find nothing to blame; only the shortness of Alexander's life and the duke's own illness frustrated his designs. He is the best model for a new prince who rises by the fortune and arms of others.

# CHAPTER VIII. CONCERNING THOSE WHO HAVE OBTAINED A PRINCIPALITY BY WICKEDNESS

Agathocles of Syracuse rose from a potter's son to king by military ability and unrestrained cruelty. He assembled the senate and people, had the senators and richest citizens killed, and seized power without civil commotion. His courage in danger and greatness of mind in hardship cannot be denied; yet his barbarous cruelty and inhumanity do not permit him to be celebrated among the most excellent men. What he achieved cannot be attributed to fortune or genius alone.

Oliverotto da Fermo, invited his uncle Giovanni Fogliani to a banquet, had soldiers issue from hiding and slaughter him and the leading citizens, then besieged the magistracy until the people obeyed him. He was destroyed only when Cesare Borgia took him at Sinigalia.

Cruelties may be called well used—if it is possible to speak well of evil—when applied all at one blow, necessary for security, and not persisted in. Badly used cruelties multiply with time. Hence the usurper ought to inflict all injuries at once so that, being tasted less, they offend less; benefits ought to be given little by little so their flavour lasts longer.

# CHAPTER IX. CONCERNING A CIVIL PRINCIPALITY

Where a private citizen becomes prince by the favour of his fellow citizens, this may be called a civil principality, requiring happy shrewdness rather than genius or fortune. In every city two parties are found: the people do not wish to be ruled or oppressed by the nobles; the nobles wish to rule and oppress the people. From these opposite desires arises either a principality, self-government, or anarchy.

He who reaches sovereignty by popular favour finds himself alone with none around him who are not prepared to obey. He cannot by fair dealing satisfy the nobles, but he can satisfy the people, whose object is more righteous—not to be oppressed. A prince can never secure himself against a hostile people, for they are too many; from hostile nobles he has to fear not only abandonment but active conspiracy.

One who becomes prince through the nobles ought above everything to win the people, for men bound more closely to a benefactor from whom they expected evil than to one who merely fulfilled their hopes. A prince ought to contrive that his citizens always need the state and him; then he will always find them faithful.

# CHAPTER X. CONCERNING THE STRENGTH OF PRINCIPALITIES

A prince who can raise a sufficient army to meet any attacker can support himself; one who cannot must shelter behind walls, fortify his town well, and manage his subjects so that he is not hated. The cities of Germany are absolutely free, own little surrounding country, are heavily fortified, keep a year's provisions in public depots, and maintain military exercises in repute—and are therefore rarely attacked. A prince who has a strong city and has not made himself odious will not be attacked; and if he is, the enemy will be driven off with disgrace, for affairs are so changeable that an army can rarely be kept a whole year in the field.

# CHAPTER XI. CONCERNING ECCLESIASTICAL PRINCIPALITIES

Ecclesiastical principalities are acquired by capacity or good fortune and held without either, being sustained by ancient ordinances of religion so powerful that the princes may behave as they will and not lose them. Before Charles VIII entered Italy, the Italian potentates kept each other in balance; the Pope was kept weak by the Orsini and Colonnesi factions. Alexander VI, using Cesare Borgia and the French, broke this balance and aggrandized the Church—though his intention was to aggrandize the duke. Julius II found the Church already strong, added Bologna, ruined the Venetians, and drove the French from Italy, doing everything to strengthen the Church and nothing for any private person.

# CHAPTER XII. HOW MANY KINDS OF SOLDIERY THERE ARE, AND CONCERNING MERCENARIES

The chief foundations of all states are good laws and good arms; where the state is not well armed there cannot be good laws. Arms are either a prince's own, or mercenaries, auxiliaries, or mixed. Mercenaries are useless and dangerous: disunited, ambitious, undisciplined, unfaithful, valiant before friends and cowardly before enemies. They have no reason to die for you beyond a trifle of stipend. The ruin of Italy has been caused by nothing else than resting her hopes on mercenaries for many years—which is why Charles VIII seized Italy with chalk in hand.

Mercenary captains are either capable, in which case you cannot trust them because they aspire to their own greatness, or incapable, in which case you are ruined in the usual way. Rome and Sparta stood for many ages armed and free. The Swiss are completely armed and quite free. The Florentines, having appointed Pagolo Vitelli their captain, found that had he taken Pisa they would have had to obey him, and if he went to the enemy they had no means of resisting. The Venetians did well so long as they fought with their own men; when they turned to mercenaries, at Vaila they lost in one day what they had won in eight hundred years.

# CHAPTER XIII. CONCERNING AUXILIARIES, MIXED SOLDIERY, AND ONE'S OWN

Auxiliaries—when a prince calls in another's forces to aid him—are even more dangerous than mercenaries, because if they lose you are undone, and if they win you are their captive. Pope Julius, having had poor proof of his mercenaries, turned to Ferdinand of Spain for auxiliaries; only extraordinary good fortune saved him from being their prisoner. The Emperor of Constantinople sent ten thousand Turks into Greece to oppose his neighbours; when the war ended they would not quit, and this was the beginning of Greece's servitude.

In mercenaries the chief danger is their cowardice; in auxiliaries, their valour. Cesare Borgia used French auxiliaries, found them unreliable, turned to the Orsini and Vitelli mercenaries, found them doubtful and dangerous, destroyed them, and turned to his own men—and was never more esteemed than when every one saw he was complete master of his own forces. No principality is secure without its own forces; it is entirely dependent on fortune otherwise. One's own forces are those composed of subjects, citizens, or dependents.

# CHAPTER XIV. THAT WHICH CONCERNS A PRINCE ON THE SUBJECT OF THE ART OF WAR

A prince ought to have no other aim or thought than war and its rules and discipline, for this is the sole art that belongs to him who rules. Francesco Sforza, through being martial, from a private person became Duke of Milan; his sons, avoiding the hardships of arms, from dukes became private persons. A prince who does not understand the art of war cannot be respected by his soldiers nor rely on them.

In peace he should addict himself to the exercise more than in war: by action, keeping men organized and following the chase, learning the nature of localities; and by study, reading histories and examining the actions of illustrious men, as Alexander imitated Achilles, Caesar imitated Alexander, Scipio imitated Cyrus. A wise prince in peaceful times increases his resources so that if fortune changes he is prepared to resist her blows.

# CHAPTER XV. CONCERNING THINGS FOR WHICH MEN, AND ESPECIALLY PRINCES, ARE PRAISED OR BLAMED

It appears more appropriate to follow the real truth of the matter than the imagination of it; for many have pictured principalities which have never been seen, and he who neglects what is done for what ought to be done sooner effects his ruin than his preservation. Hence it is necessary for a prince wishing to hold his own to know how to do wrong, and to make use of it or not according to necessity. He need not make himself uneasy at incurring reproach for those vices without which the state can only be saved with difficulty; for something which looks like virtue, if followed, would be his ruin, whilst something which looks like vice brings him security and prosperity.

# CHAPTER XVI. CONCERNING LIBERALITY AND MEANNESS

Liberality exercised in a way that does not bring you the reputation for it injures you. To maintain the name of liberal a prince is obliged to avoid no attribute of magnificence, consuming all his property, burdening his people with taxes, and becoming odious. Therefore a wise prince ought not to fear the reputation of being mean, for in time he will be more considered than if liberal—his revenues will be enough, he can defend himself, he can engage in enterprises without burdening his people. Pope Julius was assisted to the papacy by a reputation for liberality, yet he did not strive to keep it up when he made war on France, supplying his expenses out of long thriftiness. The present King of Spain would not have undertaken so many enterprises had he been reputed liberal.

If Caesar obtained empire by liberality, I answer: either you are a prince in fact, or on the way to become one. In the first case liberality is dangerous; in the second it is necessary to appear liberal. And a prince who goes forth with his army, living by pillage and extortion, handling what belongs to others, must be liberal with it—otherwise he would not be followed. There is nothing that wastes so rapidly as liberality; it is wiser to have a reputation for meanness, which brings reproach without hatred, than to incur a name for rapacity, which begets reproach with hatred.

# CHAPTER XVII. CONCERNING CRUELTY AND CLEMENCY, AND WHETHER IT IS BETTER TO BE LOVED THAN FEARED

Every prince ought to desire to be considered clement and not cruel; nevertheless he ought not to misuse clemency. Cesare Borgia was considered cruel, yet his cruelty reconciled the Romagna and restored it to peace and loyalty. He was much more merciful than the Florentines, who, to avoid a reputation for cruelty, permitted Pistoia to be destroyed. A prince who keeps his subjects united and loyal ought not to mind the reproach of cruelty; with a few examples he will be more merciful than those who, through too much mercy, allow disorders to arise from which follow murders and robberies.

Upon the question whether it is better to be loved than feared: one should wish to be both, but since it is difficult to unite them, it is much safer to be feared than loved when one must be dispensed with. Men are ungrateful, fickle, false, cowardly, covetous; as long as you succeed they are yours entirely, but when danger approaches they turn against you. Men have less scruple in offending one who is beloved than one who is feared, for love is preserved by obligation which the baseness of men breaks at every opportunity, but fear preserves you by a dread of punishment which never fails.

Nevertheless a prince ought to inspire fear in such a way that if he does not win love he avoids hatred, which he can always do by abstaining from the property and women of his subjects. Men more quickly forget the death of their father than the loss of their patrimony. When a prince is with his army he must disregard the reputation for cruelty, for without it he would never hold his army united.

Hannibal led an enormous army of many races in foreign lands without any dissension arising—owing to his inhuman cruelty, which with his boundless valour made him revered and terrible. Scipio's too great forbearance gave his soldiers more licence than military discipline permits; his army rebelled in Spain. The conclusion: men love according to their own will and fear according to the prince's; a wise prince should establish himself on what is in his own control.

# CHAPTER XVIII. CONCERNING THE WAY IN WHICH PRINCES SHOULD KEEP FAITH

Those princes who have done great things have held good faith of little account and have known how to circumvent men by craft, overcoming those who relied on their word. There are two ways of contesting: by law, which is proper to men, and by force, which is proper to beasts. Since the first is frequently insufficient, a prince must know how to use both. He ought to choose the fox and the lion: the lion cannot defend himself against snares, the fox cannot defend himself against wolves. A wise lord cannot keep faith when such observance may be turned against him and the reasons that caused him to pledge it no longer exist. If men were entirely good this precept would not hold, but because they are bad and will not keep faith with you, you are not bound to observe it with them.

It is necessary to know well how to disguise this characteristic; men are so simple and subject to present necessities that he who seeks to deceive will always find someone who will allow himself to be deceived. Alexander VI did nothing else but deceive men and always found victims. Therefore it is unnecessary for a prince to have all the good qualities enumerated, but very necessary to appear to have them—to appear merciful, faithful, humane, upright, and religious. There is nothing more necessary to appear to have than religion, for men judge more by the eye than by the hand. For that reason, let a prince have the credit of conquering and holding his state; the means will always be considered honest and he will be praised by everybody, because the vulgar are always taken by appearances and by results.

# CHAPTER XIX. THAT ONE SHOULD AVOID BEING DESPISED AND HATED

It makes a prince hated above all things to be rapacious and a violator of the property and women of his subjects. When neither property nor honour is touched, the majority of men live content, and he has only to contend with the ambition of a few. It makes him contemptible to be considered fickle, frivolous, effeminate, mean-spirited, irresolute; he should endeavour to show greatness, courage, gravity, and fortitude, and let his judgments be irrevocable.

A prince who is highly esteemed is not easily conspired against. The conspirator has nothing but fear, jealousy, and prospect of punishment; the prince has the majesty of the principality, the laws, the protection of friends and the state, and popular goodwill. One of the most efficacious remedies against conspiracies is not to be hated by the people, for the conspirator always expects to please them by removing the prince; when he can only look forward to offending them, he will not have the courage to act.

The Roman emperors had a difficulty unknown to other princes: they had to satisfy both people and soldiers, the people loving peace and the unaspiring prince, the soldiers loving the warlike, bold, cruel, and rapacious prince. Hence those who came new to the principality and could not have great personal authority adhered more readily to the soldiers. Marcus, Pertinax, and Alexander, being modest, just, and humane, came to sad ends except Marcus, who had succeeded by hereditary title and owed nothing to soldiers or people. Pertinax was created emperor against the soldiers' wishes and was overthrown at the very beginning. Severus, by contrast, knew so well how to counterfeit the fox and the lion that he kept the soldiers friendly while the people, astonished and awed, did not rise against him. His son Antoninus had excellent qualities beloved by the armies, yet his ferocity and cruelties were so unheard of that he was murdered by a centurion whose brother he had killed—a warning that a prince must not do grave injury to those he employs or has around him. Commodus gave himself up to amusing and corrupting the soldiers while indulging his rapacity upon the people, fell into contempt, and was killed. The conclusion: either hatred or contempt has been fatal to all these emperors; a new prince cannot imitate Marcus, who was an hereditary ruler, nor is it safe to imitate Severus unless he has sufficient valour to tread in his footsteps; he should take from Severus those parts necessary to found his state, and from Marcus those proper to keep a state already stable.

# CHAPTER XX. ARE FORTRESSES AND MANY OTHER THINGS ADVANTAGEOUS OR HURTFUL?

A new prince has always armed his subjects, for by arming them those arms become yours, those who were distrusted become faithful, and those who were faithful are kept so. When you disarm subjects you offend them by showing distrust, and are then compelled to turn to mercenaries. When a prince acquires a new province he should disarm it, except for those who aided him in acquiring it, and render even these soft and effeminate with time.

Fostering factions in subject towns to hold them more easily was perhaps well enough in former times, but when the enemy comes upon a divided city it is quickly lost, for the weaker party will always assist the outside forces. Such methods argue weakness in the prince.

Fortune causes enemies to arise against a new prince so that he may have the opportunity to overcome them and mount higher. Princes have found more fidelity in those who were distrusted at the beginning of their rule than in those who were trusted. Those who at the commencement were hostile, if they need assistance to support themselves, can always be gained with the greatest ease and will serve with fidelity, knowing they must cancel by deeds the bad impression formed of them.

The best possible fortress is not to be hated by the people, because foreigners will always assist a people who have taken arms against their prince. Fortresses are useful or not according to circumstances; he who has more to fear from the people than from foreigners ought to build them, he who has more to fear from foreigners ought to leave them alone.

# CHAPTER XXI. HOW A PRINCE SHOULD CONDUCT HIMSELF SO AS TO GAIN RENOWN

Nothing makes a prince so much esteemed as great enterprises and setting a fine example. Ferdinand of Aragon attacked Granada at the beginning of his reign, holding the minds of the barons of Castile occupied with war so they did not perceive he was acquiring power over them; he used the money of the Church and of the people to sustain his armies; always using religion as a plea, he drove out the Moors, assailed Africa, came down on Italy, attacked France—his achievements always great, keeping the minds of his people in suspense and admiration, never giving them time to work steadily against him.

A prince is also respected when he is either a true friend or a downright enemy, for standing neutral is always more disadvantageous than declaring yourself. He who conquers does not want doubtful friends; he who loses will not harbour you because you did not court his fate. If the party with whom you ally yourself conquers, even though he is powerful and has you at his mercy, he is indebted to you and there is a bond of amity; men are never so shameless as to oppress you with ingratitude. Victories are never so complete that the victor need show no regard to justice.

Never let any government imagine it can choose perfectly safe courses; prudence consists in knowing how to distinguish the character of troubles and for choice to take the lesser evil.

A prince ought to show himself a patron of ability, honour the proficient in every art, encourage citizens to practise their callings peaceably in commerce and agriculture, and offer rewards to whoever wishes to honour his city or state.

# CHAPTER XXII. CONCERNING THE SECRETARIES OF PRINCES

The first opinion formed of a prince is by observing the men he has around him; when they are capable and faithful he may always be considered wise. There are three classes of intellects: one which comprehends by itself; another which appreciates what others comprehend; and a third which neither comprehends by itself nor by the showing of others. The first is most excellent, the second is good, the third is useless. The one test which never fails: when you see the servant thinking more of his own interests than of yours, such a man will never make a good servant. On the other hand, to keep his servant honest the prince ought to honour him, enrich him, do him kindnesses, share honours and cares, and let him see that he cannot stand alone.

# CHAPTER XXIII. HOW FLATTERERS SHOULD BE AVOIDED

Courts are full of flatterers because men are so self-complacent in their own affairs that they are preserved with difficulty from this pest. There is no other way of guarding against flatterers except letting men understand that to tell you the truth does not offend you; but when every one may tell you the truth, respect for you abates. Therefore a wise prince ought to choose wise men, give them only the liberty of speaking the truth on things he inquires about, question them upon everything, listen to their opinions, and afterwards form his own conclusions. He who does otherwise is either overthrown by flatterers or so often changed by varying opinions that he falls into contempt. Good counsels, whencesoever they come, are born of the wisdom of the prince, and not the wisdom of the prince from good counsels.

# CHAPTER XXIV. WHY THE PRINCES OF ITALY HAVE LOST THEIR STATES

The actions of a new prince are more narrowly observed than those of a hereditary one, and when seen to be able they gain more men and bind far tighter than ancient blood. Those seigniors who have lost their states in Italy had one common defect in regard to arms, and either had the people hostile or did not know how to secure the nobles. Do not let princes accuse fortune for the loss of their principalities after so many years' possession, but rather their own sloth; in quiet times they never thought there could be a change, and when bad times came they thought of flight and not of defending themselves. Those only are reliable, certain, and durable that depend on yourself and your valour.

# CHAPTER XXV. WHAT FORTUNE CAN EFFECT IN HUMAN AFFAIRS AND HOW TO WITHSTAND HER

Fortune is the arbiter of one-half of our actions, but she still leaves us to direct the other half. I compare her to a raging river which when in flood overflows the plains, sweeping away everything; yet when the weather is fair men can make provision with defences and barriers so that the waters pass away by canal and their force is neither so unrestrained nor so dangerous. So it happens with fortune, who shows her power where valour has not prepared to resist her.

A prince may be seen happy today and ruined tomorrow without having changed his disposition. This arises because he who relies entirely on fortune is lost when it changes. He will be successful who directs his actions according to the spirit of the times, and unsuccessful when they do not accord. Two men working differently bring about the same effect; two men working similarly, one attains his object and the other does not—all arising from whether or not they conform to the spirit of the times. A man cannot deviate from what nature inclines him to, and having always prospered by acting in one way, he cannot be persuaded to leave it; hence the cautious man, when it is time to turn adventurous, does not know how to do it and is ruined.

Pope Julius went to work impetuously in all his affairs and found the times conform so well that he always met with success. Had he been required to go cautiously, his ruin would have followed, for he would never have deviated from what nature inclined him to. I conclude that it is better to be adventurous than cautious, because fortune is a woman, and if you wish to keep her under it is necessary to beat and ill-use her; she allows herself to be mastered by the adventurous rather than by those who go to work more coldly.

# CHAPTER XXVI. AN EXHORTATION TO LIBERATE ITALY FROM THE BARBARIANS

So many things concur to favour a new prince that I never knew a time more fit than the present. Italy has been reduced to greater extremity than the Hebrews under Egypt, more oppressed than the Persians, more scattered than the Athenians; without head, without order, beaten, despoiled, torn, overrun. She is ready and willing to follow a banner if only someone will raise it. Nor is there to be seen at present one in whom she can place more hope than in your illustrious house, favoured by God and by the Church of which it is now the chief.

With us there is great justice, because that war is just which is necessary, and arms are hallowed when there is no other hope but in them. Here there is great valour in the limbs whilst it fails in the head; in duels and hand-to-hand combats the Italians are superior in strength, dexterity, and subtlety, but in armies they do not bear comparison, owing entirely to the insufficiency of the leaders. It is necessary therefore, as a true foundation for every enterprise, to be provided with your own forces. There can be no more faithful, truer, or better soldiers; and when they find themselves commanded by their prince, honoured by him, and maintained at his expense, they will be much better still.

This opportunity ought not to be allowed to pass for letting Italy at last see her liberator appear. Let therefore your illustrious house take up this charge with that courage and hope with which all just enterprises are undertaken, so that under its standard our native country may be ennobled:

*Virtue against fury shall advance the fight,*
*And it i' th' combat soon shall put to flight:*
*For the old Roman valour is not dead,*
*Nor in th' Italians' brests extinguished.*

# DESCRIPTION OF THE METHODS ADOPTED BY THE DUKE VALENTINO WHEN MURDERING VITELLOZZO VITELLI, OLIVEROTTO DA FERMO, THE SIGNOR PAGOLO, AND THE DUKE DI GRAVINA ORSINI

The Duke Valentino had returned from Lombardy and was at Imola, intending to move against Giovanni Bentivogli of Bologna. The Vitelli and Orsini, fearing he would become too powerful, met at Magione in Perugia and decided to resist him; the rebellion at Urbino followed. The Florentines, from hatred of the Vitelli and Orsini, sent Machiavelli to offer assistance to the duke, who, though found full of fear and disarmed, recovered courage and decided to temporize, negotiate a reconciliation, and obtain French reinforcements.

The reconciliation was concluded: the duke confirmed former covenants, gave four thousand ducats, promised not to injure the Bentivogli, and formed an alliance with Giovanni; they in turn promised to restore the duchy of Urbino and serve him in all expeditions. But the duke had no intention of keeping this peace; he continued his preparations in secret, dispersing his troops through the Romagna in separate parties. Having assembled more than two thousand cavalry and ten thousand infantry at the Metauro, he marched toward Sinigalia, where the Vitelli and Orsini had been persuaded to wait for him—Vitellozzo very reluctantly, for the death of his brother warned him not to trust a prince he had offended, but he was overcome by the persuasions of Pagolo Orsini, whom the duke had corrupted with gifts.

The duke's trusted followers had been ordered that as soon as Vitellozzo, Pagolo Orsini, the Duke di Gravina, and Oliverotto arrived, each should be taken in hand and not permitted to leave until they came to the duke's quarters. Vitellozzo came unarmed, wearing a cape lined with green, appearing very dejected, as if conscious of his approaching death. He had recommended his house to his captains and advised his nephews to remember the virtues of their fathers rather than the fortune of their house. The leaders were received with goodwill and at once placed between those commissioned to look after them. Oliverotto, who had remained in the square drilling his men, was drawn in by Don Michele on the pretext that it was not right to keep his men out of their quarters. The whole party entered the duke's quarters, went into a secret chamber, and were made prisoners.

That night Vitellozzo and Oliverotto were strangled. Vitellozzo prayed for full pardon for his sins; Oliverotto cringed and laid all blame on Vitellozzo. Pagolo and the Duke di Gravina were kept alive until news came from Rome that the Pope had seized Cardinal Orsino and others; on 18th January they too were strangled.

# THE LIFE OF CASTRUCCIO CASTRACANI OF LUCCA

It appears a wonderful thing that all men who have performed great deeds have had their birth in baseness or been aggrieved by Fortune, so that all success must be attributed to her rather than to wisdom. Castruccio Castracani of Lucca was one of those men; born of unknown parents, found as an infant in a vineyard by Madonna Dianora, sister of the priest Messer Antonio, he was brought up by them and baptized Castruccio. He showed early that his character was quite unfitted for the priesthood, preferring arms and athletic exercises to ecclesiastical books.

Messer Francesco Guinigi, a leading Ghibelline gentleman of Lucca, observed the boy commanding his companions with a kind of royal authority, learned his story, and obtained Messer Antonio's consent to take him into his household. In a very short time Castruccio became an accomplished horseman, excelled in all exercises of strength and dexterity, and was beloved by all Lucca for his modesty with equals and courtesy to inferiors. He accompanied Francesco to Pavia in support of the Ghibellines against the Guelphs, acquiring greater reputation than any other captain, his name known throughout Lombardy.

On Francesco's death, Castruccio became governor and tutor of his son Pagolo, which increased his power but created envy, particularly from Giorgio degli Opizi, head of the Guelph party. Castruccio, fearing Giorgio might have him driven out, conspired with the exiled Ghibellines and with Uguccione of the Faggiuola, lord of Pisa. One night Uguccione approached the gate of San Piero, set fire to the portcullis, Castruccio raised a great uproar within, the gate was forced, and Uguccione entered. Giorgio degli Opizi and his family were killed, the governor driven out, and more than one hundred families exiled.

The Florentines and the Guelph party assembled a large army to restore the exiles. Uguccione fell ill and left command to Castruccio, who allowed the enemy to grow overconfident, then attacked them at a moment of his choosing, placing his most valiant men on the flanks and his less reliable men in the centre—the opposite of the enemy's disposition. His wings overwhelmed the enemy's weaker flanks while the centres remained disengaged; the Florentine centre fled when exposed. More than ten thousand men were killed, including Piero, brother of King Ruberto, and Carlo his nephew.

Uguccione, jealous of Castruccio's growing reputation, had him imprisoned on a pretext; but the Lucchese rose and demanded his release, and Uguccione, finding himself without support, fled to Lombardy and died in poverty. Castruccio, from prisoner, became almost a prince in Lucca, was appointed captain of their army, and rapidly seized Serezzana, Massa, Carrara, Lavenza, and all of Lunigiana. He then had himself created lord of Lucca by corrupting key citizens, and was solemnly elected prince by the people. The Emperor Frederick of Bavaria, coming to Italy for the Imperial crown, appointed Castruccio his lieutenant in Tuscany and lord of Pisa.

Castruccio entered into league with Matteo Visconti of Milan, organized his forces into five divisions corresponding to Lucca's five gates, and could quickly bring twenty thousand soldiers into the field. He invaded the Florentine Valdarno, seized Fucecchio and San Miniato, and forced the Florentines to recall their army from Lombardy. A rebellion by the Poggio family in Lucca was suppressed with characteristic ruthlessness: Castruccio graciously invited the rebels to submit on Stefano di Poggio's mediation, then immediately threw them into prison and put them to death. He subsequently seized Pistoia by a stratagem, entering at midnight with Pagolo Guinigi, killing both faction leaders simultaneously, and compelling the people to yield obedience.

The Florentines, assembling thirty thousand infantry and ten thousand cavalry under Carlo, son of King Ruberto, advanced against him. Castruccio with twelve thousand foot and four thousand horse chose to fight in the Pass of Serravalle, having secretly seized the castle the night before. The Florentine cavalry, mounting the hill, found Castruccio's infantry upon them before they could pull down their visors; the pass was too narrow for the army behind to deploy; in the confusion the cavalry were scattered or killed. Castruccio sent infantry round by the castle to fall on the enemy's flank; the Florentines could not sustain the attack and fled, conquered more by their position than by the enemy's valour. Many captains were taken prisoner; Pistoia surrendered immediately.

Castruccio marched into the plain of Peretola, two miles from Florence, held horse races and foot races, struck medals commemorating the defeat, and endeavoured to corrupt citizens to open the city gates at night—the conspiracy was discovered and the participants beheaded. The Florentines offered their city to King Ruberto of Naples, who sent Carlo with four thousand horsemen.

Castruccio was compelled to leave his positions before Florence to suppress a conspiracy in Pisa led by Benedetto Lanfranchi; he executed Benedetto and many noble citizens. When Carlo arrived in Florence the Florentines assembled more than thirty thousand infantry and ten thousand cavalry and marched on Pisa. Castruccio with twenty thousand foot and four thousand horse took up position at Fucecchio, between the Arno and the Gusciana, where the enemy could not approach without crossing the river. On 10 June 1328 the Florentines commenced the battle by sending cavalry and ten thousand infantry across the Arno; Castruccio attacked them before they could mount the banks, sent light infantry up and down the river to harass the crossing, and when both sides were exhausted sent fresh reserves through his own ranks—commanding his tired men to open and wheel aside—then fell upon the Florentines with renewed force. They were driven back across the river; the cavalry was then attacked by infantry and put to flight. Of the Florentines, twenty thousand two hundred and thirty-one men fell; Castruccio lost one thousand five hundred and seventy.

But Fortune, growing envious of his glory, took away his life at the moment of his greatest triumph. Standing at the gate of Fucecchio to welcome his returning men, Castruccio was exposed to the unhealthy midday wind off the Arno, took a chill, and fell into a high fever from which the doctors saw he could not recover. He called Pagolo Guinigi to him and spoke with great lucidity of the dangers that would face him: Lucca would never rest contented, Pisa was changeable and unreliable, Pistoia was disloyal and incensed, the Florentines were injured but not destroyed, the Emperor and the Milanese were far distant and slow. He counselled Pagolo to know himself and the measure of his own strength, and to make friends of the Florentines, who, suffering under their great defeat, should be inclined to listen. "It is of the greatest importance in this world that a man should know himself; and he who knows that he has not a genius for fighting must learn how to govern by the arts of peace." He died having left a vast estate, deeply concerned that he left it unsettled and insecure.

Castruccio was above the ordinary height, perfectly proportioned, of gracious presence. His hair was inclined to be red, cut short above the ears; he always went without a hat whether it rained or snowed. He was delightful among friends, terrible to his enemies, just to his subjects, ready to overcome by fraud those he desired to subdue—saying it was the victory that brought the glory, not the methods of achieving it. He was accustomed to say that men ought to attempt everything and fear nothing; that God is a lover of strong men, because the weak are always chastised by the strong. He lived forty-four years, and was in every way a prince. He died in the same year of his age as Philip of Macedon and Scipio of Rome, and would doubtless have excelled both of them had Fortune decreed that he should be born, not in Lucca, but in Macedonia or Rome.
