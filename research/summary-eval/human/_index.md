## Source Types Used

**Publisher pages** (Penguin Random House, Harriman House, Beacon Press, HarperCollins, Grand Central): Promotional, direct-address, second-person or third-person, benefit-forward. Length: 100–250 words.

**Author official sites** (jamesclear.com, ynharari.com, calnewport.com, besselvanderkolk.com, blackswanltd.com): Close to publisher copy but more personal in voice; author speaks more directly. Length: 80–200 words.

**Wikipedia lead sections**: Encyclopedic, third-person, genre-classified, includes critical reception and controversy. Length: 80–180 words.

---

## Common Structural Patterns

### 1. The Opening Move
Three distinct opener types dominate this corpus:

- **Thesis-as-provocation** (Sapiens, Never Split the Difference, Deep Work): A single bold claim designed to overturn a received belief. Example: "Everything we've previously been taught about negotiation is wrong." These are the most memorable and most distinctive of the opener types.
- **Problem → reframe** (Atomic Habits, Psychology of Money): Identifies a pain point the reader recognizes, then reattributes blame from the reader to a correctable system. Example: "If you're having trouble changing your habits, the problem isn't you."
- **Statistics + authority** (The Body Keeps the Score, Thinking Fast and Slow): Opens with arresting data or expert credentials, then explains what the book does with that authority. Example: "Trauma is a fact of life. Veterans and their families… one in five Americans…"
- **Reverence / gravity** (Man's Search for Meaning): Quotes rather than argues; borrows the book's own philosophical weight rather than explaining it. Example: leading with "He who has a why to live can bear almost any how."

### 2. Structure of the Body
After the hook, all sources converge on a predictable three-part body:
1. **What the book argues** (thesis / central claim in 1–2 sentences)
2. **How it argues it** (method, framework, structure — e.g., four laws, 19 stories, System 1/2, two-part format)
3. **What you will gain** (practical benefit, new perspective, changed behavior)

This pattern is almost universal. Exceptions are Wikipedia entries, which swap (3) for critical reception instead of reader benefit.

### 3. Closing Resolution
Publisher descriptions almost always end on a positive resolution — hope, reclamation, tools, transformation. Wikipedia ends on reception data or impact metrics. Author sites end on personal invitation. None of the strong summaries end on ambiguity or open question.

## Length Ranges

| Source Type | Typical Word Count |
|---|---|
| Publisher page blurb (full) | 150–280 words |
| Author site overview | 100–220 words |
| Wikipedia lead section | 80–180 words |
| Pull-quoted representative excerpt (1–2 sentences) | 25–60 words |

The most concise effective summaries are 100–150 words. Beyond 300 words, summaries trend toward over-explanation that dilutes the hook.

## Stylistic Hallmarks of Strong Human-Written Summaries

### Voice and Register
- **Publisher blurbs** speak to the reader directly ("You do not rise to the level of your goals") or address them as a prospective beneficiary ("will change the way you think about thinking"). The voice is confident, forward-looking, occasionally breathless.
- **Author site copy** is more intimate — often first person or close-third, treating the reader as someone already in conversation with the author.
- **Wikipedia** uses no second person, no direct address, no promises — purely declarative.

### Use of Specifics
Strong publisher summaries use concrete proper nouns and numbers to ground abstract claims: "Olympic gold medalists, award-winning artists, business leaders, life-saving physicians" (Atomic Habits); "one in five Americans has been molested; one in four grew up with alcoholics" (Body Keeps the Score); "neurons that fire together wire together" type language (though not present here, the specificity pattern holds). The specifics serve as evidence that the book is empirically grounded, not just motivational.

### Aphorism Density
The best self-help and business non-fiction summaries include at least one standalone aphorism:
- "You do not rise to the level of your goals. You fall to the level of your systems." (Clear)
- "Doing well with money isn't necessarily about what you know. It's about how you behave." (Housel)
- "He who has a why to live can bear almost any how." (Frankl via Nietzsche)
- "The real art of negotiation lies in mastering the intricacies of No, not Yes." (Voss)

These function as memory anchors — the sentence that survives from the summary long after the rest is forgotten.

### Counter-Intuitive Reframing
Across 7 of the 8 books, the most distinctive line in the summary is a reversal of common sense:
- Habits fail because of systems, not willpower (Clear)
- Humans dominate because they believe in fictions (Harari)
- We are not rational; compromise is wrong (Voss)
- Agriculture was history's largest deception (Harari again)
- Trauma reshapes the body, not just the mind (van der Kolk)
- Fast thinking is not inferior — it can also be manipulated (Kahneman)

The strongest summaries make a single counter-intuitive claim legible in plain English within the first 30 words.

### What Distinguishes Human from AI Summaries (Provisional)
Based on this corpus, human-written summaries:
1. **Commit to a specific opening gambit** — they don't hedge or provide full coverage from sentence one.
2. **Control register deliberately** — publisher copy is warm and second-person; Wikipedia is cold and third-person; author sites sit between. The register never slips.
3. **Leave things out** — strong summaries omit chapters, sub-arguments, and edge cases to preserve momentum. AI summaries tend to list comprehensively.
4. **Use rhythm as meaning** — short-short-long sentence patterns (Housel), parallel triplets (Clear), aphorism-then-expansion (Frankl) are structural tools, not decoration.
5. **Anchor to the reader's existing experience** — the best openers assume the reader has already felt the problem ("If you're having trouble changing your habits…"), which creates recognition before instruction.
