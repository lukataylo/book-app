# Atomic Habits — Human-Written Summary Sources
**Book:** Atomic Habits: An Easy & Proven Way to Build Good Habits & Break Bad Ones  
**Author:** James Clear (2018)

---

## Source 1: Penguin Random House — Publisher Description

**URL:** https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/543993/atomic-habits-by-james-clear/  
**Approximate date:** 2018 (publisher page, continuously maintained)

**Representative excerpt:**

> "If you're having trouble changing your habits, the problem isn't you. The problem is your system. Bad habits repeat themselves again and again not because you don't want to change, but because you have the wrong system for change. You do not rise to the level of your goals. You fall to the level of your systems."

**Stylistic observations:**
- Opens with a direct accusation rebuttal ("the problem isn't you") that immediately validates the reader's frustration — a classic self-help hook designed to convert skeptics.
- Relies on tight parallel aphorism structure ("You do not rise… You fall…") to make an abstract concept feel memorable and quotable.
- Uses bullet lists to enumerate concrete outcomes (habit formation, environment design, getting back on track), giving the overview a practical, checklist feel.
- Social proof ("Olympic gold medalists, award-winning artists, business leaders") is woven into the body copy rather than isolated — lends credibility without derailing the explanatory arc.

## Source 2: James Clear Official Site — Book Overview

**URL:** https://jamesclear.com/atomic-habits  
**Approximate date:** 2018 (author's official page)

**Representative excerpt:**

> "Atomic Habits is the most comprehensive and practical guide on how to create good habits, break bad ones, and get 1 percent better every day. If you're having trouble changing your habits, the problem isn't you. The problem is your system."

**Stylistic observations:**
- Tone is confident and first-person authoritative ("I do not believe you will find a more actionable book") — the author endorses his own work directly, which creates an intimate, blogger-to-reader voice consistent with Clear's newsletter brand.
- The "1 percent better every day" phrase recurs as a refrain, functioning as a mnemonic anchor rather than a statistic — rhetorical repetition as compression.
- Very short sentences predominate; ideas arrive one at a time without subordinate clauses — punchy, scannable.
- No academic hedging; claims are stated as established fact, reflecting the self-help genre's convention of confident assertion.
