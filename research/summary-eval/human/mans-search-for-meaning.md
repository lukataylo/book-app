**Approximate date:** Current (publisher's primary edition page)

**Representative excerpt:**

> "He who has a why to live can bear almost any how."

*(The Nietzsche epigraph that Frankl adopts as the book's central theme, as cited in the publisher description.)*

> "A book for finding purpose and strength in times of great despair, the international best-seller is still just as relevant today as when it was first published."

**Stylistic observations:**
- Beacon Press leads with the Nietzsche quotation as an epigraph-in-the-blurb — an unusual move that lets a third voice (Nietzsche → Frankl) carry the thesis, signaling the book's philosophical lineage before a word of synopsis appears.
- "Still just as relevant today" is a timelessness claim functioning as both endorsement and context: it tells readers the book's age without making it feel dated, and promises contemporary applicability.
- The publisher description layers endorsements from Carl Rogers ("outstanding contributions to psychological thought") and Harold Kushner ("one of the great books of our time") before summarizing the book's content — authority through citation chain.
- The description is notably brief; it positions the book more as an object of reverence than a product to be sold — befitting a work now treated as a literary-philosophical classic rather than a self-help release.

- "The New York Times describes it as 'an enduring work of survival literature'" — the NYT quote does critical genre-classification work in five words: "survival literature" names a category precise enough to signal tone and gravity without summarizing the Holocaust.
- The logotherapy definition is rendered as a crisp two-clause contrast: "the primary human drive is discovering and pursuing what individuals find meaningful, rather than seeking pleasure" — Freud as foil, efficiently dispatched.
- Ending on "finds significance in the act of living, despite obstacles" is deliberately open-ended — the generality invites application to any reader's circumstances, which explains the book's longevity across very different historical moments.

## Source 3: Wikipedia — Lead Section

**URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Man%27s_Search_for_Meaning  
**Approximate date:** Article last reviewed ~2024

**Representative excerpt:**

> "Man's Search for Meaning is a 1946 autobiographical work by Viktor Frankl documenting his experiences as a Nazi concentration camp prisoner during World War II, and describing his psychotherapeutic method, which involved identifying a purpose to each person's life through one of three ways: the completion of tasks, caring for another person, or finding meaning by facing suffering with dignity."

**Stylistic observations:**
- The Wikipedia sentence is unusually long and information-dense: a single sentence covers genre, date, author, subject matter, the theoretical method, and its three sub-categories — comprehensive but heavy.
- "Autobiographical work" rather than "memoir" or "book" — Wikipedia's genre precision signals that the text combines lived experience with therapeutic theory, which is distinctive.
- The three-part logotherapy taxonomy ("completion of tasks, caring for another person, or finding meaning by facing suffering with dignity") appears in the opening sentence — the taxonomy is treated as load-bearing enough to go in the lead, not tucked into a later section.
- The Library of Congress "10 most influential books" citation provides a third-party impact measure that neither publisher uses — Wikipedia draws on institutional rather than commercial metrics for significance.
