## Source 1: Penguin Random House — Publisher Description

**URL:** https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/89308/thinking-fast-and-slow-by-daniel-kahneman/  
**Approximate date:** 2011 (publisher page, maintained)

**Representative excerpt:**

> "The guru to the gurus at last shares his knowledge with the rest of us. Nobel laureate Daniel Kahneman's seminal studies in behavioral psychology, behavioral economics, and happiness studies have influenced numerous other authors, including Steven Pinker and Malcolm Gladwell. In Thinking, Fast and Slow, Kahneman at last offers his own, first book for the general public. It is a lucid and enlightening summary of his life's work. It will change the way you think about thinking."

**Stylistic observations:**
- "The guru to the gurus" is a striking opening gambit — establishing authority through a second-order endorsement (the authority that other authorities defer to) rather than a credential list.
- "It will change the way you think about thinking" is a double-loop payoff line — the reflexivity of the phrase enacts the book's subject matter, which is thinking about cognition itself.
- Named influences (Pinker, Gladwell) are cited not as endorsers but as downstream students — a subtle inversion that positions Kahneman above a tier of already-famous authors.
- The description shifts smoothly from authority-building → content summary → practical promise ("practical and enlightening insights"), following the classic publisher arc: who this person is → what they've done → what you'll get.

## Source 2: Wikipedia — Lead Section

**URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thinking,_Fast_and_Slow  
**Approximate date:** Article last reviewed ~2024

**Representative excerpt:**

> "Thinking, Fast and Slow is a 2011 popular science book by Israeli-American psychologist Daniel Kahneman. The work presents a foundational concept dividing human cognition into two distinct systems: System 1 — 'fast, instinctive and emotional' — and System 2 — 'slower, more deliberative, and more logical.'"

**Stylistic observations:**
- Genre identification ("popular science book") comes in the first sentence alongside the disciplinary label for the author ("psychologist") — Wikipedia's efficient two-bit context-setting.
- The System 1 / System 2 framework is presented as a typology with brief parenthetical definitions — functional rather than evocative, prioritizing clarity over readability.
- Signals intellectual lineage: "much conducted with colleague Amos Tversky" contextualizes the work within a collaborative research program, giving historical depth not present in publisher copy.
- Notes the replication-crisis controversy around cited priming studies — responsible for the book's unusual position in the canon: simultaneously celebrated and partially contested. Wikipedia is the only source here to flag this.
