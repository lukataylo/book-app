**Named scene/person/study:** None in `summary_short`. The Amish technology-trialling example, the thousand-person 30-day experiment, and Abraham Lincoln's cottage retreat all appear in `summary` but are absent from the short. `summary_short` names Newport and references "slot machines" as a mechanic analogy only.

**(a) 4 — Thesis lands, but framing delays it.**
"Your screen-time report describes hours you never consciously chose to spend" opens on reader experience, not a claim. The thesis arrives in the second sentence ("the fight between your willpower and your apps was rigged from the start"), which is strong. Loses one point for the detour through reader-identification before stating the argument.

**(b) 2 — All mechanism, no scene.**
The slot-machine mechanic and "unpredictable rewards" are explained abstractly. No company, no named study, no number. The long summary names New York publishers' 1830s attention model and Newport's thousand-volunteer experiment; neither appears here. "Like slot machines" is simile, not evidence.

**(c) 2 — Pure advocacy; one inflated signpost.**
Offending text: *"Newport also mounts the book's most original argument around solitude."* No stated limit anywhere — Newport is a computer-science professor, not a psychologist; the solitude-deprivation link to anxiety is asserted but the underlying evidence is contested and worth one caveat. Nothing in the text says who this philosophy suits best or where it strains.

**(d) 2 — No burst; uniform mid-length.**
Shortest complete sentence is 9 words. The third paragraph is one 52-word sentence. No sub-8 sentence anywhere. Four em-dashes at 4.5/300 displace what could be short sentence breaks.

**(e) 2 — Multiple tells.**
4 em-dashes (4.5/300); "book's most original argument" inflated signpost; 3-item tricolon in close ("the attention economy's history, the declutter and reintroduction rules in detail, and Newport's complete case"); 3-item list in paragraph 3 ("strenuous hobbies, things built with your hands, structured social commitments").

**(f) 1 — Worst close in this batch.**
Pure table-of-contents: "The full summary walks through the attention economy's history, the declutter and reintroduction rules in detail, and Newport's complete case for reclaiming your free hours." Lists three of our content sections, says nothing about the book. **Surgical edits:**
1. ### drive — 18/30

**Scores:** (a) 5 | (b) 2 | (c) 4 | (d) 3 | (e) 2 | (f) 2

**Measurements:** ~272 words · 4 em-dashes · 4.4/300 · avg sent 24.7w · shortest 7w ("Pink is practical about the limits.")

**Meta-close:** YES — *"The full summary unpacks the experiments behind each claim, the company case studies, and Pink's field instructions for managers, teachers, parents, and your own motivation."*

**Named scene/person/study:** Weak. "Lab monkeys solving puzzles for the fun of it" refers to Harry Harlow's 1949 experiment (named in `summary`) — in `summary_short` it is anonymous. "Students whose interest evaporated once payments stopped" refers to Edward Deci's puzzle study and the preschool drawing experiment (both named in `summary`) — anonymous here. Atlassian and Google are named in `summary` but not the short. "The research he cites" is vague attribution.

**(a) 5 — Best hook in this batch.**
"Pay people more for creative work and they often perform worse — that strange, well-replicated finding sits at the heart of Daniel Pink's Drive." Counterintuitive, specific, provocative. The thesis follows without throat-clearing.

**(b) 2 — Lab monkeys go unnamed.**
The opening finding (Harlow's monkeys, Glucksberg's candle puzzle, Deci's college students, the preschool drawing study) are the backbone of chapter 2 of `summary` and exactly what give this claim teeth. In `summary_short` they are reduced to "decades of research" and "lab monkeys." The reader cannot verify or visualise the experiments.

**(c) 4 — Genuine friction paragraph.**
"Pink is practical about the limits." is a sub-8 sentence that signals the review is coming. The paragraph that follows states real limits: rewards work for routine work; baseline pay must be fair before anything else applies; unexpectedly delivered recognition differs from a dangled carrot. This is the strongest friction section in the batch. Loses one point because there is no acknowledgment that the research rests heavily on lab tasks and may not replicate cleanly in high-stakes real-world incentive settings.

**(d) 3 — One sub-8 sentence; Autonomy/Mastery/Purpose paragraph is a structural tell.**
"Pink is practical about the limits." (7 words) breaks the rhythm correctly. However, the second paragraph is listicle-in-prose: "Autonomy: [40-word sentence]. Mastery: [40-word sentence]. Purpose: [30-word sentence]." Each colon-label-then-clause reads as a bullet with the hyphen removed. This is the same pattern flagged in `deep-work` in batch 1.

**(e) 2 — Tricolon-heavy; em-dashes above threshold.**
4 em-dashes (4.4/300); the Autonomy/Mastery/Purpose structure IS itself a tricolon; "narrow focus, drain intrinsic interest, and invite shortcuts and gaming" (3-item); "managers, teachers, parents" (3-item in meta-close); "the experiments behind each claim, the company case studies, and Pink's field instructions" (3-item meta-close). Five three-or-more-item lists.

**(f) 2 — Meta-close + tricolon.**
"The full summary unpacks the experiments behind each claim, the company case studies, and Pink's field instructions for managers, teachers, parents, and your own motivation." Exactly the formula. Better close candidate: *"Pink's most uncomfortable implication is that most of us have spent years pulling levers that work in the wrong direction — not because we were lazy or malicious, but because we were using a textbook written for a different century."*

**Surgical edits:**
1. Name Harlow and one experiment in sentence 2: *"Pink's anchor is a 1949 experiment: Harry Harlow found his lab monkeys solving mechanical puzzles for no reward — and then getting worse at them once he started paying with raisins."* (verify against book)
2. Convert the Autonomy/Mastery/Purpose listicle into a through-line sentence: *"The replacement system runs on three human needs: the wish to steer your own work, the drive to get better at something that matters, and the pull toward a purpose beyond the paycheck — and the research suggests most incentive systems actively suppress all three."*
3. Replace the meta-close with the book's sharpest implication (see above).

### essentialism — 12/30 (worst in batch)

**Scores:** (a) 3 | (b) 1 | (c) 1 | (d) 3 | (e) 2 | (f) 2

**Measurements:** ~273 words · 4 em-dashes · 4.4/300 · avg sent 30.3w · shortest 6w ("The discipline starts with reclaiming choice.")

**Meta-close:** YES — *"The full summary develops each of these moves with his examples, from the closet test for opportunities to the graceful exit from sunk costs."*

**Named scene/person/study:** None. No named individual, no named study, no specific number or statistic. The "closet test" is a concept illustration; it is not a scene from the book with a person, setting, or outcome. The Silicon Valley career-collapse paradox discussed in `summary` never surfaces. The Amish and the ninety-app observation appear in `summary` as anecdotes but are absent here.

**(a) 3 — Locates the reader; does not state the thesis.**
"Greg McKeown wrote Essentialism for the capable person whose reward for good work is an avalanche of other people's priorities." This is reader-identification, not a provocation. The actual thesis — *energy concentrated beats energy diffused* — appears in sentence 3 and is competently stated, but the first sentence is setup, not argument. The SKILL rule is "first two sentences state the book's core claim."

**(b) 1 — No concreteness at all.**
This is the only `summary_short` in the batch with zero named persons, zero named studies, and zero numbers. "The capable person," "others," "your calendar" — everything is second-person generic. The long summary has the closet-audit example (useful), the Amish technology-trialling comparison (vivid), the journalist-exploration framing, and specific Silicon Valley career stories. None reach `summary_short`. This is the most severe concreteness failure in the batch.

**(c) 1 — No friction anywhere.**
Pure advocacy from first sentence to last. No stated limit. No for-whom. "The place where the philosophy starts costing something" (refusal section) is the closest thing — it acknowledges social friction but treats it as a pure win ("long-term respect"). Essentialism is a philosophy that actively suits people who already have professional leverage to say no; it strains for junior employees, caregivers, and anyone without institutional authority to decline. None of that appears.

**(d) 3 — One sub-8 sentence exists; everything else is long.**
"The discipline starts with reclaiming choice." (6 words) is a clean rhythm break at the start of paragraph 2. The success→options→commitments triplet using semicolons ("Success, he observes, breeds options; options breed commitments; commitments diffuse the very focus that produced the success.") is technically varied in unit length but is a mechanical pattern — the same structure three times. The 4-item refusal list in paragraph 2 is the flattest passage.

**(e) 2 — 4-item list; em-dashes above threshold; meta-close.**
4 em-dashes (4.4/300). The 4-item refusal list: *"buy time before answering, separate the decision from the relationship, offer a smaller alternative, deliver a soft no with a hard boundary"* — a comma-separated imperative list with the bullets removed. The semicolon triplet in sentence 2 is a structural pattern tell. Meta-close is softer than most ("his examples, from the closet test...") but still redirects to our content.

**(f) 2 — Soft meta-close; no book motivation.**
"The full summary develops each of these moves with his examples, from the closet test for opportunities to the graceful exit from sunk costs." This is slightly softer than the standard formula but still points at our content. Nothing in the close makes you want the book itself.

**Surgical edits:**
1. Replace the opening with a named Silicon Valley scene: *"Greg McKeown watched something strange happen to talented people in Silicon Valley: the better they performed, the more invitations arrived, until the success they had built was entirely consumed by serving other people's priorities. That is not bad luck. It is a predictable trap — and Essentialism is his way out."* (verify the Silicon Valley framing against the book; `summary` confirms this)
2. Add the concreteness the short desperately needs — the closet-audit illustration belongs in the body, not the meta-close: *"His standard filter asks not 'could I conceivably use this?' but 'do I genuinely love and use it?' Apply that question to your calendar and most of what is there fails immediately."*
3. Add one friction sentence: *"The philosophy is most powerful for people with professional leverage to decline — it strains for anyone who cannot afford to say no without consequences. McKeown acknowledges the 'graceful exit' costs something; he is less candid about who can afford the bill."* (needs source check: confirm this is reviewer's observation, not invented from the book)

The quiz scene is real and described (named audience types, real outcome). "Four income levels" is the book's central framework and is at least gestured at here. **(d) 2 — No sub-8 sentence; modest variance.**

**(e) 3 — Cleanest on em-dashes in the batch; three tricolons.**
Only 3 em-dashes (3.6/300), nearest to the acceptable range. But three tricolons: "bankers, ministers, and Nobel laureates"; "villains, looming threats, and a world split cleanly into us and them"; "the remaining instincts, the four income levels in vivid detail, and the small repeatable habits" (meta-close). "The full summary works through the remaining instincts, the four income levels in vivid detail, and the small repeatable habits that make a worldview check its own expiry date." Standard three-part recipe. That is the tone the book earns, and the summary cannot carry it."*

1. Add one specific quiz result: *"When Rosling asked audiences what percentage of the world's one-year-olds are vaccinated against common diseases, the median guess hovered around 20 percent. 2. Add a Level 2 daily-life detail in the income-levels sentence: *"On Level 2 — around four dollars a day — a gas canister replaces gathered wood, children have sandals, and a bicycle opens a wider labor market. Most of the world's people live here, invisible to anyone still using the word 'developing.'"* (verify against book)
3. Replace the meta-close with the possibilist close (see above), cutting the tricolon formula.

### flow — 16/30

**Scores:** (a) 5 | (b) 3 | (c) 1 | (d) 3 | (e) 2 | (f) 2

**Measurements:** ~281 words · 6 em-dashes · 6.4/300 · avg sent 31.2w · shortest 7w ("They arrived mid-effort, absorbed in a challenge.")

**Meta-close:** YES — *"The full summary adds the cross-cultural case studies, the diagnosis of why leisure disappoints, and how the model doubles as a repair manual for stale pursuits."*

**Named scene/person/study:** Partial. Csikszentmihalyi and his pager study named and described. "Accident victims who lost the use of their bodies described rebuilding their lives as profoundly meaningful" — real finding from the book (the paralysis interviews) but not named. The assembly-line worker who turned his station into a private sport (one of the book's best concrete illustrations) appears in `summary` but not the short. No named individual in `summary_short`.

**(a) 5 — Research-scene hook.**
"Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi paged thousands of people at random moments and asked how they felt — and discovered that their best moments almost never arrived in comfort." Concrete methodology, named researcher, counterintuitive result. Excellent.

**(b) 3 — Study named; no individual named.**
The pager study is concrete (named, described, real). The paralysis-recovery finding is real but genericised ("accident victims"). The long summary includes a named assembly-line worker example that would add a second scene; the `summary_short` doesn't have room but could replace one of the long fingerprint-list sentences. Awarded 3 rather than 2 because the pager methodology is specific enough to picture.

**(c) 1 — Pure expansion; "even catastrophe bends to the model."**
Offending text: *"Even catastrophe bends to the model."* This is the opposite of friction — it is the claim that the framework is limitless. There is no acknowledgment that the pager methodology is self-report; that flow's prerequisites (clear goals, immediate feedback) are much easier to engineer in some jobs than others; that the challenge-skill balance framing has not replicated as cleanly as Csikszentmihalyi believed. The strongest friction in the long summary is a mild one ("decades of pager beeps went into these ideas") — it doesn't appear in the short.

**(d) 3 — One sub-8 sentence; 7-item list flatlines.**
"They arrived mid-effort, absorbed in a challenge." (7 words) is a genuine rhythm break, the best sentence-length moment in this pack. But paragraph 2 contains: *"The state has a recognizable fingerprint: clear goals, immediate feedback, action merging with awareness, a sense of control, self-consciousness evaporating, time warping, and the activity becoming its own reward."* — a 7-item list (the longest in the batch) that is also written in -ing gerund form, which amplifies the listicle feel. Six em-dashes convert several would-be short sentences into parenthetical appendages.

**(e) 2 — 6 em-dashes; 7-item list; "subversive finding" signpost.**
6 em-dashes (6.4/300). The 7-item fingerprint list is the worst single list in the batch. "The pager data also yields a subversive finding" is a mild inflated-significance signpost. 3-item meta-close tricolon.

**(f) 2 — Meta-close + tricolon.**
"The full summary adds the cross-cultural case studies, the diagnosis of why leisure disappoints, and how the model doubles as a repair manual for stale pursuits." Standard formula. Better close: *"Csikszentmihalyi's sharpest finding is the one people ignore: sampled in the moment, they report more flow at work than in the free time they spend the week longing for. The book is an explanation of that gap — and a set of tools for closing it."*

**Surgical edits:**
1. Collapse the 7-item fingerprint list to two or three essentials: *"The state has a signature: you always know what to do next, feedback arrives continuously, and at some point the gap between the dancer and the dancing disappears."* This cuts the listicle, adds an image, and still names the three defining conditions.
2. Add the assembly-line-worker example (from `summary`): *"One of Csikszentmihalyi's subtler examples is a factory worker who converted a repetitive station into a private sport — timing himself, refining each movement, finding daily absorption in work most people would call deadening."* (verify against book)
3. Kill "even catastrophe bends to the model" and replace with one friction sentence: *"The model assumes you can engineer clear goals and immediate feedback. Many jobs — caregiving, creative work, long-cycle research — resist both, and Csikszentmihalyi is more useful as a diagnostic tool than as a prescription in those domains."* (needs source check)

### four-thousand-weeks — 15/30

**Scores:** (a) 5 | (b) 2 | (c) 2 | (d) 2 | (e) 2 | (f) 2

**Measurements:** ~283 words · 5 em-dashes · 5.3/300 · avg sent 28.3w · shortest 16w (†)

**Meta-close:** YES — *"The full summary expands each argument, including his rehabilitation of settling, the case for having problems, and why this is really a philosophy book wearing a productivity jacket."*

**Named scene/person/study:** None. Rod Stewart's model-railway hobby (one of the book's most charming concrete moments) is absent. No philosopher is named (Heidegger, Bergson). No specific productivity system or author is named. Burkeman's own columnwriting background is described generically ("years as a productivity columnist").

**(a) 5 — Titular provocation works.**
"Live to eighty and you get roughly four thousand weeks — a number small enough that, Oliver Burkeman argues, the entire productivity industry exists to stop you from feeling it." The arithmetic in the title does the work; the hook earns its place.

**(b) 2 — No named scene from the book.**
The arguments are all stated correctly, but none are anchored to a person, place, or named case from the text. The book's most memorable illustration — Rod Stewart spending "meticulous, unprofitable, gloriously pointless hours" on model railways — is entirely absent. The "corridor habit" example (parents missing their child's childhood by postponing enjoyment) appears in `summary` but not here. Both would add one named reference each without requiring invention.

**(c) 2 — "It will not" is Burkeman's own claim, not friction.**
"It will not, and the deeper error is treating time as a substance you possess and spend." This is bold but it is the book's thesis, not an editorial caveat. There is no acknowledgment that Burkeman's frame is strongest for people with middle-class discretionary time, or that finitude acceptance can be used to rationalise inaction. The meta-close tail — "why this is really a philosophy book wearing a productivity jacket" — is the summary's only meta-critical moment, and it appears in the close where it cannot inform the reader's engagement with the body.

**(d) 2 — No standalone sub-8 sentence.**
"It will not, and the deeper error…" continues with a comma, making it an 18-word sentence rather than a 3-word statement. "The weeks are not the budget for your life; they are the life itself." is 16 words total; the second clause (5w) follows a semicolon, not a period. No period-terminated sub-8 sentence exists. Five em-dashes (5.3/300) absorb the energy that should power short-sentence rhythm breaks.

**(e) 2 — Em-dashes above threshold; tricolon close; corridor-device overused.**
5 em-dashes (5.3/300). The corridor metaphor (present→degree→promotion→renovation) is effective but repeated twice in the third paragraph. 3-item meta-close tricolon. "His sharpest diagnosis" is a mild inflated-significance signpost.

**(f) 2 — Meta-close + tricolon.**
"The full summary expands each argument, including his rehabilitation of settling, the case for having problems, and why this is really a philosophy book wearing a productivity jacket." The "philosophy book wearing a productivity jacket" tail is the sharpest phrase in the whole summary and belongs in the open or the close of the pack itself, not in the meta-close formula. Better close: *"Burkeman's most discomfiting move is rehabilitating the word settling — arguing that since every conceivable life involves trade-offs, the only question is whether you settle consciously, for something, or accidentally, for nothing in particular. That question does not fit in a summary."*

**Surgical edits:**

## Issues common to all 10 packs

**1. The meta-close formula: 10 of 10.**
Every `summary_short` ends with "The full summary [verb] …" followed by a three-part table of contents of our own content. This is the catalog's single most systematic defect, present in every pack in batches 1 and 2 (18 of 18 so far). The fix is the same each time: delete the formula, close on the book's sharpest implication or the open question only the book can answer.

**2. Em-dash over-density: 9 of 10.**
Only factfulness (3.6/300) is near the acceptable range. Mean across the other nine is 5.6/300, more than five times the ~1/300 target. The pattern is identical across packs: em-dashes are used to attach clarifying clauses rather than to mark genuine reversals. Each attached clause is an opportunity for a short sentence that is being converted into a long one. The worst offenders are grit (7.4/300), getting-things-done (6.5/300), and emotional-intelligence (6.5/300).

**3. No authorial friction: 8 of 10.**
Only good-to-great and drive carry a genuine stated limit. The other eight advocate unconditionally. The pattern is structural: friction, where it exists at all, is deferred to the meta-close formula ("an honest look at how the science has aged," "Duckworth's caveats about where persistence shades into vice") — which means the reader never encounters it in the body of the text. One sentence per summary naming who the book serves best, where the evidence strains, or what is contested would move the mean (c) score from 2.0 to approximately 3.5 across the batch.
