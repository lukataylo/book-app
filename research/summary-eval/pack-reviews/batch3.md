`indistractable`, `influence`, `letters-from-a-stoic`, `made-to-stick`, `make-time`,

### 1. guns-germs-and-steel

| Criterion | Score |

**(a) Hook — 5.** "In 1532 a few hundred Spaniards brought down an empire of millions"
opens on a named historical scene and lands the thesis (geography, not biology) within
the same breath. Excellent.

**(b) Concreteness — 4.** The Fertile Crescent named with specific crops ("wheat, barley,
and legumes"); Eurasia's "east-west axis" vs. Americas' "north-south" orientation; New
Guineans named. One gap: the 1532 opening doesn't name Pizarro or Atahualpa, leaving
the most famous scene in the book without its protagonists.

**(c) POV — 4.** Genuine friction: *"Critics counter that he underweights culture,
institutions, and chance, and that debate continues."* Diamond's method is interpreted,
not just reported. Strong for this criterion.

**(d) Rhythm — 3.** Twelve sentences (best count in batch), average 27.9 words, two
sentences at exactly 8 words: *"It was the land they happened to inhabit."* and
*"Continental shape then decided how fast advantages traveled."* These are the borderline
cases — 8 words, not sub-8. The rhythm is the most varied in the batch but
technically doesn't meet the sub-8 criterion. No flatline.

**(e) AI-tells — 3.** Five em-dashes at 4.48/300 (above threshold). Both appear as
paired clarifying asides:
> *"the familiar explanations that reduce to biology — that some peoples are innately
> smarter or more industrious — are wrong"*
> *"keep asking why behind every proximate cause — guns and steel trace back to farming,
> farming to plants and animals, plants and animals to geography — until the moralizing
> falls away"*

Eight tricolon-ish lists — the joint-highest count in batch 3. No inflated signposts.

**(f) Close — 2.** *"The full summary walks each link of the chain in detail, from the
tests an animal must pass to be tamed to why disease flowed in only one direction."*
Standard meta-close with a content tease. The book's actual closing implication (the
argument was built specifically to dismantle the idea that conquest reveals who deserved
to win) goes unused.

**Long summary close — PASS.** Ends: *"Read it for the discipline of refusing easy answers,
for the thrill of watching thirteen thousand years explained by starting conditions, and
for the moral weight of an argument built specifically to dismantle the idea that
conquest reveals who deserved to win."* Motivates the book. No meta-close.

**Offending text:**
- Meta-close formula: *"The full summary walks each link of the chain in detail…"*
- Paired em-dash asides (two pairs = 4 of the 5 dashes)
- Tricolon density: *"wheat, barley, and legumes"* / *"soldiers, scribes, and inventors"*
  / *"meat, milk, plows, and cavalry"* / *"plants and animals to geography"* across 335 words

**Edit instructions:**
1. Name Pizarro and Atahualpa in the opening sentence: *"In 1532, Francisco Pizarro
   led fewer than two hundred Spaniards to capture the Inca emperor Atahualpa and
   topple millions."* (needs source check: both names are in Diamond's book; Cajamarca
   is the location — verify the 200-man figure)
2. Convert the two paired em-dash asides to subordinate clauses or parentheses; cut the
   dash count from 5 to 2.
3. Replace the meta-close with the argument's sharpest moral implication: the same
   starting-condition logic applies to today's inequalities — poverty is not punishment
   but geography compounded across centuries.

**(a) Hook — 4.** Names Grove and Intel and delivers the thesis ("a manager exists to
produce output") in two sentences. Credentials-then-premise structure — solid but not
a scene. No concrete incident from Grove's career as the hook.

**(b) Concreteness — 3.** The "breakfast service" analogy is Grove's own opening device
from the book (genuine) but is a metaphor, not a named real-world case. No named Intel
milestone, no named executive whose output Grove measured, no study with figures. The
"one-on-one" meeting type is named but undefined in the short.

**(c) POV — 3.** *"a contrarian defense of meetings"* is mild interpretation. No stated
limit: Grove's output-measurement model was built for semiconductor manufacturing and
may strain for creative knowledge work where goals are ambiguous and timescales are long.
No friction sentence.

**(d) Rhythm — 2.** Ten sentences averaging 33.1 words; shortest is 12 words:
*"Grove's first move is to treat all work as a production process."* No sentence under 10
words. The prose is uniformly long and declarative — a textbook rhythm flatline. Every
sentence carries multiple attached clauses.

**(e) AI-tells — 3.** Five em-dashes at 4.53/300 (above threshold). The meta-close
itself adds a four-item em-dashed list:
> *"The full summary unpacks each piece of this machinery — the breakfast factory,
> honest delegation, the meeting types, and the review that improves future performance
> instead of merely rating the past."*
Inflated signpost: *"the book's most famous idea, leverage"* — state what leverage means
without the "most famous" framing. Six tricolon-ish lists.

**(f) Close — 2.** *"The full summary unpacks each piece of this machinery — the
breakfast factory, honest delegation, the meeting types, and the review that improves
future performance instead of merely rating the past."* Pure meta-close with a
four-item table of contents.

**Long summary close — PASS.** Ends: *"Grove ran one of the hardest businesses on earth
and wrote down exactly how; the least a working manager can do is read the manual."*
Strong. Redirects to the book, not the app. Has edge.

**Offending text:**
- Meta-close: *"The full summary unpacks each piece of this machinery…"*
- Signpost: *"the book's most famous idea, leverage"*
- No short sentence in 331 words

**Edit instructions:**
1. Open with Grove's breakfast factory example directly rather than credentials:
   *"Andy Grove opens by asking how you would run a three-item breakfast — egg, toast,
   coffee, all hot, on time. The answer turns out to be the same answer as how you run
   Intel: find the bottleneck, catch defects early, measure output not activity."*
   Then deliver the thesis.
2. Cut "the book's most famous idea" — write: *"This produces leverage: every managerial
   act carries a multiplier…"* State it; don't rank it.
3. Plant one short sentence after defining leverage — *"The math is unsentimental."* (5 words) — and replace the meta-close with Grove's sharpest claim: that the quiet manager whose team ships reliably is more productive than the star who personally closes five deals.

**(c) POV — 3.** The "group project" observation at the close is a genuine limit: *"always-on
workplaces are systems failures dressed up as personal weakness."* But no stated limit on
who the four-layer system works for — it assumes autonomy over one's calendar that shift
workers or hourly employees don't have. No friction sentence naming that gap.

**(d) Rhythm — 4.** Shortest sentence: *"Eyal's second move is definitional."* (5 words).
Good variation. The 5-word standalone sentence creates a real punch before the longer
definitional paragraph.

**(e) AI-tells — 2.** **Eight em-dashes in 331 words = 7.25/300.** This is the highest

> joyful — that you want as the day's centerpiece"* (two em-dashes in one sentence)
> *"The full summary expands the whole menu — choosing highlights, the distraction-free
> phone, the energy tactics, and the nightly notes…"*

Seven tricolon-ish lists. Meta-close. The em-dash density is the pack's defining tell.

**(f) Close — 2.** *"The full summary works through all four layers in sequence, plus
Eyal's advice for offices, kids, and the friends who keep their phones on the dinner
table."* Pure meta-close.

**Long summary close — AMBIGUOUS.** *"Read the full version for the research notes, the
workplace and parenting chapters that summaries inevitably shortchange, and the worksheets
that turn the model into a week-by-week practice."* "The full version" is ambiguous
in-app — could be the full book or the longer in-app summary. The payoff sentence that
follows (*"It is a sturdier answer to the question of whether you can trust yourself with
your own attention"*) is strong and book-oriented.

**Offending text:**
- Meta-close: *"The full summary works through all four layers in sequence…"*
- Paired em-dash list: *"urgent, deeply satisfying, or simply joyful — that you want as

  the dread of a hard task"* — another em-dash where a colon belongs

**Edit instructions:**
1. Cut at least five em-dashes: convert colons and parentheses:
   *"Every distraction begins with an internal trigger — boredom, anxiety, the dread of a
   hard task"* → *"Every distraction begins with an internal trigger: boredom, anxiety,
   a task you've been dreading."* Apply the same conversion to the highlight-type list
   and the close.
2. Name a research source behind the internal-trigger or ten-minute-delay techniques —
   Eyal draws on acceptance-based therapy and urge-surfing techniques developed by G. Alan
   Marlatt (needs source check: Marlatt's urge surfing is cited in Indistractable; verify)
3. Replace meta-close with Eyal's most counterintuitive claim: that the problem is
   never the technology — a person who cannot sit in a queue without reaching for their
   phone is already distracted before the phone exists.

## Issues common to all 10 packs

### 1. Meta-close — 10 / 10 (universal; trivially fixable)

Every summary_short ends with a sentence pointing at our longer summary or the "full
version," usually packaged as a three- or four-item table of contents:

- *"The full summary walks each link of the chain in detail, from the tests an animal
  must pass to be tamed to why disease flowed in only one direction."* (guns-germs)
- *"The full summary unpacks each piece of this machinery — the breakfast factory,
  honest delegation, the meeting types…"* (high-output-management)
- *"The full summary adds the six likability habits in detail, the machinery of
  correcting people without breaking them, and a frank accounting of which criticisms
  of the book have aged well…"* (how-to-win-friends)

This pattern is the catalog's most consistent single defect and the easiest to remove.
Delete the formula. End on the book's sharpest consequence or its open question.
The long summaries in this batch mostly already do this (8 of 10 long summary closes are
product-free and book-motivated) — copy that energy back into the short.

### 2. Em-dash over-density — 9 / 10 (near-universal; mechanical fix)

Nine of ten summary_shorts exceed the ~3/300w threshold. Batch average: **5.3/300.**
Worst: `indistractable` (7.25), `mastery` (6.19), `letters-from-a-stoic` (6.09).
Only `made-to-stick` (2.74) is clean.

The pattern is always the same: em-dashes used to attach clarifying lists
(*"one activity — urgent, satisfying, or joyful — that you want…"*), not for dramatic
reversal. The fix is mechanical: convert two-thirds of all dashes to colons, commas, or
parentheses. Reserve one em-dash per piece for a genuine tonal break.

### 3. Rhythm flatline — 6 / 10 (majority; one-sentence fix)

Six of ten packs have no sentence under 12 words; three of those (`mans-search`,
`mastery`, `high-output-management`) have nothing under 22 words. The SKILL.md
threshold is a sub-8-word sentence per ~200 words. The fix is cheap: one planted short
sentence per 200 words, placed after a long one for contrast. The long summaries in this
batch are generally better on rhythm — their section titles act as natural short beats.
The summary_short has no structural equivalent.
