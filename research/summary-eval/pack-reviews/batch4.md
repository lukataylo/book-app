### measure-what-matters — 17/30

**Scores:** (a) 4 | (b) 3 | (c) 2 | (d) 4 | (e) 3 | (f) 1

**Measurements:** 348 words · 5 em-dashes · 4.31/300 · avg sent 26.8w · shortest 4w ("The mechanics are simple.")

**Meta-close:** YES — *"The full summary explores each superpower in depth, along with the case studies from Google, Intel, and the Gates Foundation that teach the judgment behind the rules."*

**Named scene/person/study:** Partial. Andy Grove at Intel named; "famously pitched to Google's young founders in 1999" is a named moment. No founder names (Larry Page, Sergey Brin), no specific company result. The Gates Foundation appears only in the meta-close.

**(a) 4 — Hook, mostly works.**  
"John Doerr's core provocation in Measure What Matters is that organizations rarely fail for lack of good ideas — they fail for lack of disciplined execution." The twist lands cleanly. Loses a point for the book-attribution wrapper ("John Doerr's core provocation in Measure What Matters is that…") — SKILL says "no throat-clearing."

**(b) 3 — Named but not witnessed.**  
Andy Grove is named; the 1999 Google pitch is named. Both are context, not scene. No concrete outcome is quoted ("Google grew from X to Y under OKRs"). The summary explains what OKRs *are* rather than showing them *working*.

**(c) 2 — The book's caveat, not the reviewer's.**  
"he insists the framework only amplifies a culture that is already honest rather than repairing one that is not" — this is a valuable caveat, but it is Doerr's own claim, reported faithfully. There is no independent editorial voice asking who this system fails, whether the Intel/Google data generalises, or where OKR adoption has backfired.

**(d) 4 — Rhythm is the best quality here.**  
"The mechanics are simple." (4 words) cuts cleanly into a long paragraph. Good variance (range 4–40w). The em-dashes are the rhythm's enemy — five of them break sentences mid-clause rather than letting short sentences do that work.

**(e) 3 — Above-threshold em-dashes; serviceable otherwise.**  
5 em-dashes (4.31/300), above the ~1/300 ideal. Two are used correctly as reversal markers; three are clause-attachment ("organizations rarely fail for lack of good ideas — they fail for lack of disciplined execution"; "it has a price — writing down only three…"). The formula sentence "CFRs — continuous conversations, feedback, and recognition —" uses both dashes to define a single acronym; a colon or parentheses would be cleaner. "Doerr's most underrated warning" is a mild inflated-significance signpost.

**(f) 1 — Classic table-of-contents meta-close.**  
Offending text: *"The full summary explores each superpower in depth, along with the case studies from Google, Intel, and the Gates Foundation that teach the judgment behind the rules."* A three-item list of our content. Fails the trailer test.

**Surgical edits:**
1. Open with a scene, not an abstraction. The book's strongest evidence is Google: name the outcome. Suggested replacement for sentence 1–2: *"When John Doerr walked into Google in 1999 and pitched OKRs to Larry Page and Sergey Brin, the company had a few dozen employees and no revenue model. Within a decade it was the most valuable internet business on earth. Doerr's argument since then has been simple: organizations fail not from a shortage of ideas but from a shortage of disciplined execution."* (needs source check: confirm Google adopted OKRs at that meeting and the timeframe)
2. Replace the CFR definition em-dash pair with a colon: *"he proposes CFRs: continuous conversations, feedback, and recognition."*
3. Kill the meta-close. Replace with the book's sharpest warning: *"The system has one hard precondition: an OKR organization with dishonest leadership will become a very efficient way to pretend."* (needs source check: verify this is Doerr's own framing or close to it before attributing)

Compare the body-keeps-the-score pack which names the ACE study.

**(a) 3 — Thesis submerged under a cliché.**  
Offending text: *"Carol Dweck's decades of research turn on a deceptively simple question: what do you believe ability is?"* "Deceptively simple" is the catalogue's most-flagged AI cliché (see `eval-results.md`: never-split-the-difference). The question itself is fine, but the thesis — that the two belief systems predict divergent outcomes — is not stated as a claim in the first two sentences. The provocative form would be: *"Carol Dweck's central finding is that the belief 'ability is fixed' is a trap, and that nearly every parent and teacher who wants to encourage a child accidentally sets it."*

**(b) 2 — Near-zero concreteness.**  
The summary describes the theory's implications without a single anchor from the book: no named child, no named classroom study, no number (e.g., "the process-praised group improved their scores by X points while the intelligence-praised group's scores fell"), no named athlete or executive. "children praised for intelligence began avoiding hard problems" is accurate but generic. **(c) 4 — Best friction score in the batch.**

"since nobody holds a pure growth mindset" is a honest caveat. Best-in-batch on this criterion.

**(d) 2 — Worst rhythm in the batch.**  
All 12 sentences run 10–52 words; the shortest at 10 words ("The two beliefs diverge most sharply around effort and failure") is still well above the SKILL sub-8 threshold. Average sentence length 30.1 words. No burst sentences anywhere. The prose is uniform — "literary" flatline.

**(e) 2 — Several tells converge.**  
"deceptively simple" opener (AI cliché); 4 em-dashes (3.32/300); 11 three-or-more-item lists by count (highest in batch alongside meditations); meta-close tricolon. The sentence "The same logic dismantles the romance of the natural — performers celebrated as born geniuses usually hid enormous appetites for practice" is a strong idea buried in a long subordinate clause.

The phrase "your own defaults" is the kind of warm but vague promise that invites skipping.

**Surgical edits:**
1. Rewrite the opener to remove the cliché and lead with a scene. The famous praise study is the right anchor: *"In Dweck's lab, two groups of children solved the same problems. Researchers praised one group for their intelligence; they praised the other for their effort and strategy. Then they offered everyone a harder puzzle. The intelligence-praised children mostly declined. The process-praised children mostly said yes. That gap — in a five-minute exchange — is the engine of Mindset."* (needs source check: verify the exact study design from Dweck 1998/Mueller & Dweck)
2. Add one short sentence after the longest paragraph to break the flatline. After "a grade of not-yet keeps you on a learning curve where a verdict of failure ends it," insert: *"The word matters."* (5 words)
3. Kill the meta-close. End on the book's sharpest implication: *"Dweck's uncomfortable conclusion is that every time you tell someone they are brilliant, you may be training them to avoid the next hard thing."*

**Scores:** (a) 5 | (b) 5 | (c) 3 | (d) 2 | (e) 1 | (f) 1

**(b) 5 — Rich and specific.**

"Automatic retirement enrollment dramatically raised participation among the very workers who said they wanted to save and never got around to it" is almost as good as a scene. **(c) 3 — Addresses the manipulation charge, but as the book's defence.**  
"a nudge that takes an hour on hold to escape has curdled into coercion" is a genuine limit-test. "no architect should deploy a nudge they would be unwilling to defend in public" is the book's own transparency rule. Shortest measured sentence: 19 words. Average: 32.2 words. The em-dashes create choppiness rather than relief — they subdivide long sentences into even-longer compound structures. **(e) 1 — Worst AI-tell score in the batch.**

**(f) 1 — Meta-close with embedded dash list.**

2. Replace the Sweden pension counter-example (which only appears in the `summary`) with a single sentence here: *"When Sweden let citizens opt out of the default pension fund into their own choices, they piled into recently-soaring tech stocks and went on to underperform the default they'd rejected — the reminder that better choice architecture sometimes means a better default, not more options."* (needs source check: verify Sweden pension privatisation details are accurate before including)
3. Kill the meta-close. End on the book's most actionable close: *"The quiet punchline is that you already hold the job. Every form you design, every menu you arrange, every default you choose — that is choice architecture. The only question is whether you are doing it on purpose."*

## Issues common to all 10 packs

### 1. Meta-close: 10 of 10 (universal)
Every `summary_short` ends with "The full summary…" followed by a table-of-contents list pointing at our product. No exceptions. The formula is present even in the highest-scoring packs. This is the single most mechanical, most visible AI-factory tell in the catalogue — and the easiest to fix: delete the formula sentence and replace it with the book's sharpest unresolved implication.

Note: ALL 10 `summary` fields close correctly by recommending the *book*, not our summary. The meta-close problem is exclusively a `summary_short` defect.

### 2. Em-dash over-density: 9 of 10 (near-universal)
Only meditations (1.76/300) is within the SKILL threshold (~1/300). The remaining nine average approximately 4.9 em-dashes per 300 words. The dominant pattern across the batch is **paired-dash list-embedding**: using "— item A, item B, and item C —" to insert a parenthetical list inside a long sentence instead of writing a shorter sentence or using a colon. This pattern is particularly acute in nudge (7.63/300), predictably-irrational (6.02/300), and nonviolent-communication (5.97/300). Fix: convert paired-dash lists to colons or commas; reserve em-dashes for one genuine reversal per summary.

### 3. Rhythm flatline, concentrated in 4–5 packs
At least four summaries have no standalone sentence under 13 words (mindset, nudge, outliers, peak). Two more (outlive, predictably-irrational) have exactly-8-word sentences that just miss the sub-8 threshold. The underlying cause is structural: long sentences subdivided by em-dashes *feel* varied because of the internal punctuation, but the sentence-length distribution is flat. The fix for each is simple — one injected short sentence per ~200 words: "The mechanics are simple." (measure-what-matters gets this right); "Repetition is not practice." (peak could use this); "The word matters." (mindset).
