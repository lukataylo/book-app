- **(d) 4:** 13 sentences ranging 10–54 words; the most burstiness in this batch.
- **(e) 3:** 4.78 em/300; seven 3+-item lists; one hedge adverb ("controversially"); meta-close tricolon.
- **(f) 2:** "The full summary traces each revolution, the great unifiers, and the happiness ledger in depth." Pure table-of-contents recap; the closing question in the paragraph before it ("Sapiens now wields god-like powers to redesign life itself without any settled idea of what to want") is the real close and gets buried.

**AI-tells flagged:**
> "controversially counts liberalism and capitalism" — "controversially" is a hedge adverb that signals to readers the writer is nervous; name the debate instead.
> "the happiness ledger in depth" — opaque without context; a non-reader gets nothing from it.

**`summary_short` close check:** YES — meta-close formula. The sentence immediately before it ("and Sapiens now wields god-like powers to redesign life itself without any settled idea of what to want") would make a far stronger final line.

**`summary` close:** Good — "Sapiens rewards readers who like big arguments they can argue back at: historians quarrel with plenty of its details, and that quarrel is part of the value." No meta-close formula in the long summary.

**Surgical edits:**

2. Delete the final sentence. End on "Sapiens now wields god-like powers to redesign life itself without any settled idea of what to want." That is the book's sharpest implication and it's already written; it just isn't the last line.
3. Drop "controversially" before "counts liberalism and capitalism" — state the claim flatly: "He places liberalism and capitalism alongside traditional religions: each rests on values that can be believed but not proven."

## Issues common to all 10 packs

**1. The meta-close formula — 10 of 10.**
Every `summary_short` ends with "The full summary…" or "the full summary…" The sentence redirects to our longer product rather than to the book. Easiest mechanical fix in the catalog: delete the formula, end on the book's sharpest implication. Notably, in 7 of these 10 packs, the `summary` (long) close does NOT use the formula and lands well — which means the writers already know how to write a strong close, and are adding the formula on top of it. Remove it from `summary_short` and the close quality rises immediately.

**2. Em-dash over-density — 9 of 10 above the 3/300 flag line.**
Only principles (3.33/300) is borderline; seven-habits (8.70) and radical-candor (7.38) are severely above. The dominant pattern is the parenthetical pair — em-dash, clause, em-dash — which appears at least once in 7 of 10 packs, almost always enclosing a list. The fix is structural, not lexical: recast parentheticals as a new sentence or a colon; reserve one em-dash per piece for a genuine dramatic reversal.

**3. No friction / no stated limit — 8 of 10.**
Only sapiens (agricultural revolution as "questionable bargain"; liberalism as a belief system) and rich-dad-poor-dad (rich-father controversy; leverage risk) supply a stated limit. The other eight are unconditional advocacy. This is the highest-leverage quality gap in the batch because it costs one sentence per summary and is what most separates a human editor's voice from a model's. Each pack should carry one sentence identifying who the idea serves, where the evidence strains, or what critics contest.
